# Unprepared

Settling into parenthood was really difficult. I hadn't accounted for the extent of the fatigue and the hormones that sent my brain and body haywire. More than this, though, the emotional turmoil was at an all-time high.

No one took me seriously as a mum. When we took her to the doctor for sniffles and the like, I was pulled through the wringer about whether I was feeding her properly, or what my home situation was like. Oh, now you deign to speak to me directly, I wanted to grumble to the doctor, but I bit my tongue. Once, the doctor actually stripped off my baby's clothes, presumably to check for bruises or sores as a result of neglect.

When I battled with exhaustion, it was written off because I'd 'made my bed', and now I had to lie in it.

If only I could lie in bed! I thought.

Struggling with a cheating partner was supposedly reasonable because, well, he was 'also so young'.

I was snubbed at moms and tots groups. My feedback and experiences were overlooked. I was patronisingly placated when I showcased feelings that 'normal' mums experienced, like fluctuating hormones, tears, and hardship.

I had fucked up, and no one was going to let me forget it.

Perhaps I deserved it. I had made choices with overwhelming implications for everyone nearest to me. If I hadn't 'made my bed', they wouldn't have had to lie in it, either.

Yes, the difficulties were byproducts of the choices I'd made, but the constant exclusion and mistrust made me wonder whether I would ever be able to truly atone for my mistake. When would I be considered capable enough to be reintroduced into society?

I wasn't old enough to be a 'real' mum.

I was no longer youthful enough to be accepted by other teenagers.

I wasn't experienced enough to be included by other mums.

I wasn't independent enough to support my child.

I felt like I was in limbo.

Although young, I had all the same whirlwind experiences of becoming a new mum that others had. I had the same 'WTAF' response when the hospital nurse grabbed my nipple and shoved it into my little one's mouth to get her to latch. I, too, cried (and felt sorry for myself) when it became sullenly apparent that motherhood didn't come as naturally as everyone said it should.

Everyone around me seemed to assume (or outright suggest) that my age was the reason I wasn't coping. I was 'too young to be a mother'. For many months, I wondered whether this was the explanation for my initial inability to bond with my daughter.

It wasn't my age that made motherhood hard. It was the silence, the condescension, the immense expectations with no reassurance.

I now realise that age had nothing to do with it. I reckon that we simply aren't all wired with inherent maternal instincts. They can take time to develop.

But, at the time, I worried that there was something wrong with me because, by and large (mostly large), I didn't find any of it as profound, gratifying, or miraculous as many women described it.

Perhaps none of us are truly equipped for birth and motherhood.

And yet, we're expected to just figure it out.

None of the prenatal classes had prepared me for the fuckery that ensued from pregnancy to birth and beyond.

The only thing they taught me is that there really should be an option for delivery by way of courier, because neither C-section nor 'natural' seemed like a birth plan anyone should have to choose.

I need a soapbox; I have pertinent questions to pose.

Why the fuck did I have to watch videos of wailing women from the 60s who clearly had never seen a razor (or even garden shears)?

No one in these classes warned me that my big tummy wouldn't miraculously disappear once the watermelon had vacated it.

I knew breastmilk would come from my nipples, but no one mentioned that they would leak so profusely that – along with changing my baby 65 times a night – I would also have to change my button-up breastfeeding nighty (sexy) because I, too, was wet, cold, and crabby.

No one informed me that the hospital-issued, hairnet-like post-delivery panties are so ridiculously shit and that I would be fine using normal panties (preferably stretched, soft, holey ones), or that 'maternity' pads are just preposterous, and I could have purchased good old Always Ultra-Thin nighttime pads instead (or use the leftover ones from the periods my parents thought I was having for the three months when I hadn't told them I was pregnant).

No one prepared me for the magnitude of the sleep deprivation. I was told that my little one would need to feed every four hours or so. I was not told that the feeding routine, when done thoroughly, would take nearly three hours (feed; wake her up to finish the feed; burp; change babygrow now covered in vomit, change wet nappy; change nappy a second time after she immediately fills the clean one with shit; feed her on the other boob; potentially have to change her nappy another fucking time; then rock her to sleep for 30 minutes... on a good night) and that in the single remaining hour between feeds, I wouldn't be able to sleep anyway because the new sounds emanating from the cot were so foreign that I needed to check constantly that my kid wasn't suffocating herself to death.

Why was I not told??

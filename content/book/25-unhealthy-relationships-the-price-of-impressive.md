# The Price of Impressive

The situationship with Tinder Guy (because let's not pretend it was anything more) was starting to rub me raw – and not in the good way.

I was getting tired of crumbs.

Tired of scraps disguised as generosity.

Tired of pretending the ache in my heart was excitement.

I needed commitment, and it wasn't forthcoming.

My daughter still hadn't met this man who consumed my heart and headspace. It cut me deeply. Despite his early theoretical interest in her, he hadn't shown any hint of desire to actually get to know her.

And why would he? There was no reason to. He didn't owe anything to anyone.

The more he pulled away, the more I ran after him.

(Metaphorically, obviously. We have already established that I am no good at actual running.)

In my overwhelming yearning to have him want to commit to me, I made some fairly life-altering decisions. I decided that he would take me more seriously if I was more... impressive.

Maybe he would respect me if I displayed the typical indicators of success – you know: house, car with sunroof, good job, sexy body, etc. So I set about acquiring these things, starting with a home of my own.

I figured that moving out would make it more convenient for him to see me, because I wouldn't have to formulate lies to facilitate a rendezvous. He could simply show up whenever he had the chance.

I was convinced that if I did this – if I made it easier for him – then he would want to be in a relationship exclusively with me.

Sane, remember?

I pictured a white picket fence. A tyre swing for my daughter. The kitchen window framing us locked in a tender kiss – a living portrait to hang in my heart.

However, being a single mum with a kid in a private school, my budget was so miniscule that the only place I could afford to rent was a tiny, rustic cottage on a smallholding on the outskirts of my hometown. It didn't have a picket fence, a tree, or place to park the pavement-mounting car with the sunroof that I also didn't have, but I was so excited to have a place that my daughter and I could call our own, and even more excited that Tinder Guy could come over whenever he wanted, so that we could feel more like a couple.

More like a family.

The actual move was easy, since I had almost nothing to move. I didn't own a single item besides clothes and toiletries.

I had to acquire absolutely everything: furniture, fridge, curtains, microwave, washing machine, dishwasher, bedding, towels. My parents kindly purchased and passed on what they could; however, the bulk of the stuff I bought on credit.

Tiny budget, remember?

Wise.

A little voice began to whisper some strange shit in my head: 'He won't want you if you use those old bowls with the faded blue flowers that your mum gave you.'

I panicked. What should I do, then?

'Carol Boyes,' the voice whispered.

Because nothing says emotionally stable like R600 salad tongs.

And I didn't stop at homeware. That scheming voice told me I also needed Country Road jeans, and a Michael Kors handbag, and Jimmy Choos (I'd reneged on my never-again deal with high heels).

After signing my soul away to the bank, my daughter and I moved into our new home.

It actually felt pretty great. I felt like a real adult. I could cook what I wanted when I wanted. I could tidy up if and when I felt like it. I could put all my impressive new Carol Boyes stuff where I wanted it. I could pack the dishwasher however I wanted (not with the Carol Boyes stuff, obviously. That was strictly handwash only. I really made things easy for myself). Best of all, I could make decisions for my daughter and myself without feeling the pressure of onlookers.

The independence was liberating.

The independence was also expensive.

Impressiveness? Even more so.

Who knew being a functional grownup was so pricey??

Unfortunately, the credit card company didn't accept impressiveness as payment; no matter how exceptional I felt, they demanded cold, hard cash.

My budget was now so tiny (and further squeezed by debt repayments and crippling interest) that I wasn't able to feed both my daughter and myself. I usually told her that I'd had a big lunch at work (a blatant lie) or wasn't really hungry (ditto). If she left anything on her plate after dinner, then I would eat. At least this had the upside of maintaining one of the criteria for success: sexy bod – tick.

See? Impressive.

I managed to keep up the pretence for some time. If Tinder Guy came for dinner, I always made something marvellous (and then prayed he didn't finish it all, so that I could freeze the leftovers so that my kid could eat for the rest of the week).

If we went out and it was my turn to pay, I used my credit card.

It was always my turn to pay.

I planned little getaways for him and me, because I knew this was a way to get him alone without distraction. This meant more credit card debt.

I was certain that if I could prove to him how impressive I was and how well I managed and conducted myself, he would choose me.

I developed a psychologically unhealthy desire to serve him. I considered it a privilege to be able to love him and spend money on him.

(Yes, I know...)

Unfortunately, this wasn't reciprocated. There was more than one occasion where I cooked a roast or some other impressive, expensive meal and he simply failed to pitch up. More than one occasion where I had paid for a babysitter and planned a special outing for us, only for him to call me – hours after I expected him – to tell me that he couldn't make it. Sometimes he didn't even call, and I sat alone in a fancy restaurant or on a park bench with a lavish picnic basket, waiting longingly for his arrival.

So impressive.

But, eventually, all the waiting and all the emotional trauma paid off.

Nearly a year and a half after we'd met and had that instant, electric connection, he announced on a naughty weekend away (which I had organised, paid for, and lied to my folks and kid about, saying it was a work trip) that he was ready to commit.

To me.

Only me.

I was delirious with happiness.

We were officially exclusive.

A real couple.

It had all been worth it.

All the hurt, all the disappointment, all the blood tests to confirm I hadn't caught some dreaded STD, hadn't been for nothing.

We entered a new era of bliss.

He came to dinner every night. He met my daughter and made the effort to develop a relationship with her, playing with her and encouraging her. If I needed help around the cottage, like fixing appliances or moving furniture, he was there to do it with me. He loved me even harder than he did before.

He bought groceries and helped with the cooking. We danced in the kitchen together, while my daughter showed us her moves (thank goodness not the sexy-hands-in-the-air, touching-her-underboob kind). We all sat at the table and ate together, and for the first time since my daughter was born, I looked around me – and didn't see what was missing.

I saw us.

My impressive little family.

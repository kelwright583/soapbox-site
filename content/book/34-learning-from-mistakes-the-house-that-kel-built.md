# The House that Kel Built

Just like that, it all came crumbling down.

This time around, the hurt wasn't fiery and forceful.

It was cold and bone deep and made me feel weak, like flu pains.

It took over me, settled in quietly, and spread through my body.

I felt devastated. Like a house flattened by a wrecking ball.

Dilapidated.

Derelict.

I was so flipping tired.

I just wanted to sleep.

All. Of. The. Time.

The joy of life had been smashed out of me.

I didn't give a shit about my job. (I mean, I gave a shit about not starving to death. Like, I wanted the paycheque, and the bonuses. A raise would have been great, too. I just didn't want to have to work for it.)

I kept getting sick. I was put on three courses of antibiotics in less than a year.

I couldn't stand being near my daughter, because I felt like a failure. It was too difficult to face a virtuous little human who was looking up to me for cues on positive relationships, when I clearly couldn't give her any.

I knew that I needed to get my arse into gear because she was counting on me, but I was just empty. Instead of setting an example of a brave, grownup woman who is self-assured and in control of her life, I simply sat in the wreckage of myself, helplessly poking at the rubble with a stick.

If we're really going to do this metaphor justice (or overdo it completely – either suits me), I had no energy or inclination to think about rebuilding my demolished 'house'. I'd rather have stabbed my eye out with that stick.

I didn't know where to start.

I didn't have the enthusiasm to post sassy updates on socials.

#singleagain

#mensuck

#boyssuck

#noregrets

Scratch that.

#heapsandheapsofregrets!

I didn't have the zeal to pull out my virtual soapbox and tell the world how I'd been wronged (which should have been the first sign that I was in a bad headspace).

I didn't even have the strength to write an outraged essay to the Afrikaner. (Yep, definitely in a bad headspace).

The pain felt different this time.

When Tinder Guy had wronged me, I'd had energy. Negative energy, but energy all the same.

Remember that?

I'd been buzzing with anger. I'd felt potent. I'd taken action. I'd done everything in my power to try to create a version of me that wasn't hurt, or broken, or struggling – then I could con myself into believing that I was whole.

But perhaps I'd been a couple of window frames short of a house the whole time.

When I thought that Tinder Guy couldn't love me because I was riddled with a snag list of shortcomings, I went on a mission to change the fittings and fixtures (including those downstairs, if you recall). The problem was that I didn't really repair anything. I simply painted over the cracks.

Also, the rationale behind this 'paint job' was faulty. It was all about trying to get him back. Or, at the very least, to make him regret rejecting me.

Yep.

Let's be honest. My motivation hadn't been about healing. It was totally about trying to win him back.

Crap.

This time around, I had no energy to do anything whatsoever.

Zero.

Nothing.

Fok-all.

I was tired of the same thoughts rattling around in my brain (despite the fact that I didn't seem to be making much use of my brain, I did still have one).

For so long, I'd been genuinely convinced that if I lost weight, if I used enough spray tan, if I somehow managed to get my nipples to point in the same direction (preferably not south), then men would like me.

If I could just be skinny enough, he would be faithful.

If I could just be wealthy enough, he would take my opinion seriously.

If I could just have the perfect vagina, he would cherish my body.

If I could just do, and say, and be enough, he would commit to me.

He would prioritise me.

He would love me.

Only me.

I now know that that desperation was my biggest mistake.

I also now know that we all make mistakes. Some bigger than others.

Fortunately (she says, selflessly forcing herself – for the sake of those less worldly-wise – to climb onto her figurative soapbox and standing as tall as her literal high heels will allow), mistakes can teach us valuable lessons. They give us the opportunity to feel deeply, to reflect, and to grow. Dwelling in the shame of my own stupidity had gotten me nowhere in the past, so I didn't bother to feel ashamed now.

Rather than blaming him (or blaming myself), I began to see the situation for what it was.

Without the energy for emotion, I could focus on cold, hard facts.

We were together.

Then we weren't.

Cars have four wheels.

Tax is mandatory.

I weigh 70 kg.

I lie about my weight.

We've run out of milk.

It is 12 o'clock.

The weather is mild today.

Imagine you failed a math test when you were in Grade 3 (sorry, I have to use you in this example, because I was good at school and would never have failed a Grade 3 math test). Thinking back on it now, would you wail and hang your head and rend your garments? Would you sob, 'I failed the test. I'm a failure. I will never amount to anything.'?

I bloody well hope not.

My guess is that you would say, 'Yep, I bombed it,' and move on.

Because you're no longer emotionally invested in the Grade 3 math mark. Since then, you have either improved your mathematical ability, or you have realised that your talents lie elsewhere.

Maybe we can't change facts, but over time we can learn to control how we respond to them.

Sometimes coulda-woulda-shoulda isn't helpful.

I'm not sure that there is ever an end to healing, but (in my case, at least) over time, the crippling anxiety and hurt became dispassionate memories of something that had happened to me in the past.

Sometimes I still had moments of delusion where I fantasised that the Afrikaner would pack up his whole life in Cape Town and come find me, and like, have my babies and stuff.

He would show up with a bouquet of Bougainvillea and a small rock.

On a ring.

A three-carat rock.

But I knew that those were just romanticised illusions of grandeur, like a mirage of perfection that hovers on the highway of life on really hot days.

I had to build my 'home', and wasn't keen to build it on a highway, because smog and noise and general ick.

So I tried not to make a judgement call when I spoke of what happened.

The things simply were the way they were.

The only thing left to do was carry on.

One useful thing the Afrikaner did teach me was the term 'fok voort'.

Fuck forward.

So forward I fucked, through the rubble of my life.

As I kicked through the debris, I had a moment of revelation.

I realised that I was the architect of my life. The faults in the foundations had happened on my watch.

For years, I'd been so focused on all the exterior crap – what I looked like, what I earned, what other people thought of me – that I hadn't noticed that my values, my personality, and inherent excitement and joy had been slowly disintegrating.

And trying to redecorate a house when its foundations have long since disintegrated is a blueprint for disaster.

I needed to find a new way to heal.

Without gym (or Jim).

Without hair dye.

Without intimate cosmetic surgery.

I was sick to death of covering up my brokenness by giving my façade facelifts. I wanted to restore the parts in me that were broken beneath the surface, brick by broken brick.

I was tired of living in a house of cards. I wanted to erect a house built of something sturdier than laminated cardboard. A house with less Jokers.

Well, if I had to rebuild anyway, I figured, I might as well do some remodelling.

A nice, big reno, like on Extreme Makeover: Home Edition. (I spent a lot of time on the couch at this point watching reality TV shows, okay?)

I was going to bash down internal walls. Open the space up.

Maybe put in a Jacuzzi.

This time, I'd build with care. No anxious fumbling for whatever materials I could lay my hands on. No harried paint jobs to cover up faults.

If I was truly going to reconstruct the ruins of my 'house', I needed to start working on the inside.

Yes, I needed to work on loving my insides.

Ew.

Let me try that again:

I needed to work on loving myself, on the inside.

Right after I slept for a few months.

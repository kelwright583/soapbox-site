# Bundle of Joy

This story began with me staring down at my day-old daughter and wondering what the fuck I'd done. I had no idea of the challenges that lay ahead of me – as a mother, as a daughter, as a lover, and as an independent young woman trying to navigate her way through an ever-changing world whilst maintaining a semblance of dignity and a tidy vagina.

We've come a long way since then, my daughter and me.

She really is my bundle of joy.

There are times when she is a bundle of 'you've got to be freaking kidding me' or a bundle of hormonal sass in overpriced jeans, but the joy overrides the rest.

I'm awed by how much we've both changed and grown.

She now has hair, on a head full of beautiful ideas. Instead of being sprawled out on my hospital bed all teeny and temperamental, she now spreadeagles across my queen-sized one, hogging the duvet and irritating the crap out of me by squeezing the fat on my arms while we binge-watch old episodes of shows that have somehow raised us both.

Those hands – no longer tiny, but still captivating – don't live in her mouth anymore. They live in motion: tapping beats, creating art, holding space for people who need softness.

They're expressive, warm, powerful.

But it's her heart that astounds me most.

Despite a precarious beginning, her heart has stayed open, supple, generous. It overflows with a love so vast and persistent that it's shaped my healing more than any therapy or mountain summit ever could.

She has grown into a beautiful and inspiring young woman.

And me?

I've grown too.

Not just in girth (though let's not pretend that didn't happen), but in grace.

In perspective.

In purpose.

I guess you could say I didn't shrink from purpose.

Incepto ne Desistam.

My old headmistress would be so proud.

If life is a dance, I've learnt, it doesn't need to be choreographed by the guilt-tripped ghost of your former self.

It's okay if it's awkward.

Off-beat.

Barefoot.

Improvised.

It's not about the performance; it's about the courage to keep moving anyway.

To live without feeling you need to earn it.

To forgive yourself for the flab and the failing.

To laugh in the midst of heartbreak, and to boldly declare it from whichever soapbox might hold your weight and lift your important voice above the noise.

Because, when the lights come on, the DJ packs up, and the perverts go home – you do too.

You toss the red heels in the back of the cupboard, wipe off the smudged makeup, and return to the rhythm of real life: the place where you can shimmy with your dignity intact. The place where you can sprawl on the bed with your daughter, tangled in blankets and belly-laughs, watching any old average-to-middling rerun – because the content doesn't matter. What matters is that, for a moment, you forget the aches of the past and remember what it feels like to be home.

Maybe it's about letting go of the woman you're trying to be – and coming home to the one you already are.

Because, really, when the last fuck has flown, that's what life is about.

I learnt this the hard way.

I twirled too fast.

I tripped.

I freestyled.

I danced with the wrong partner.

I nip-slipped.

I fell on my full arse.

But I got back up (with several expletives and all the grace a curvy girl can muster).

I occasionally ran offstage for a cry in the bathroom or a costume change, but I returned.

And in the grand finale, I rocked that stage.

Not for the audience, but for me.

The star of my own performance.

Centred.

Sweating.

Shining.

Smiling.

No makeshift soapbox.

No mic drop.

No curtain call.

I finally decided to play the lead in my own life – where my own applause was all I needed.

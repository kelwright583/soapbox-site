# Foreign Bodies

This thing that had been part of me for the last nine months was now a separate little being. She was no longer connected to me. When I held her in my arms – this little foreign body with all its squeaks and leaks – I felt protective over and in love with her, but I also felt so disconnected from her and from myself.

My folks and I took her for all the necessary checkups and progress appointments. She was weighed, measured, assessed, documented, and vaccinated. Her development was right on schedule, and her growth was always in line with the graph.

I was asked about my ability to breastfeed and about my sleeping patterns, but no one ever asked how I was doing. I suppose no one considered that I might have had postnatal depression. Hell, I didn't even consider it myself.

What does a 17-year-old know about postnatal depression?

I was tired, frustrated, teary, and listless. But didn't all mums feel like this when they had a new baby?

I had heaps of milk. I was able to get up for feeds and changes. My daughter was faring well, and yet, somehow, I was miserable.

I loved her so damn much, but I felt upset, angry, and perplexed about the future.

There were times when I would look at my little human in her cot, watch her cry, and feel devastated that she was so beside herself. Yet I just couldn't make an attempt to console her. As if it wasn't worth me trying, because I knew that I wouldn't be able to do it. I felt like a failure.

The disconnect I felt toward my child spilled over into other areas of my life. My new body was one of these areas. It felt entirely foreign.

Let's start with my boobs. I'd been primed about the milk. I knew that normal mums sometimes 'leaked'. My boobs, however, literally sprayed like fire hoses. I had to angle my kid's face so that her mouth was just above my nipple and be on the ready so that when I opened the flood gates (i.e. removed the breast pad), she wouldn't get milk in her eyes, on her face, and all over the hundredth babygrow I had changed her into that day.

I should have used Always Ultra-Thins on my boobs instead. I'd have cut down the changing time by half.

My stomach, meanwhile, was a loose bag of jiggles barely connected to my body. With every bump and jolt, it hurdled over my hairnet panties and sprawled across my substantial thighs like a suicidal sloth.

But even worse than these physical changes, my emotions felt like they weren't my own. I was permanently teary, permanently anxious, permanently offended, and I felt like all I did was wait until the next time she cried, until the next feed, until the next nappy change, until the next day, until she grew up.

I was wishing my – and her – life away.

Perhaps the antidote would have been to focus on the positive aspects of having a baby, but here's the agonising confession: I couldn't think of any.

People couldn't wait to tell me how wonderful motherhood was. How special it was. How grateful I should be. The excessive positivity made me want to puke.

I will personally kick the soapbox out from under anyone who is prepared to stand there and honestly proclaim that it is all flipping marvellous.

Let's just admit, out loud, that there are downsides to having children.

I mean, they can be like little parasites. From the second they are conceived, they deplete our bodies of nutrients (hence the need for all those expensive prenatal vitamin supplements). They then come into the world (after having taken your body to hell in a hand basket) and begin demanding.

Demanding milk.

Demanding affection.

Demanding food.

Demanding toys.

Demanding play dates.

Demanding stories.

Demanding birthday parties.

Demanding tooth money.

Demanding later bedtimes.

Demanding the newest iPhone.

In fact, if I were to look at a child as I would an investment opportunity, I wouldn't need to be an actuary to determine that having one would exhaust all my reserves – financial, physical, and emotional.

Wow. I've been wanting to say all that for a while!

Nonetheless, on the odd occasion when I wasn't overcome with sheer exhaustion and stress, I could admit that, despite it all, children could be the best investment ever. Even in the midst of my despair, somewhere deep in my mum-bones, I knew that my daughter would generate returns far greater than I could ever have imagined. I knew that she was an exquisite masterpiece. I knew that her life was the product of God's plan. I knew that having her was the ultimate gift and testament to grace.

I just couldn't convince my brain to focus on this knowledge.

I couldn't understand it. From the second I saw her on my first scan, I was blinded by the extent of my love for her: an inexplicable, unconditional, unjaded, unequivocal love which defied all logic. So why did I find myself, again and again, staring blankly at her as she cried in her crib? Why couldn't I flipping pick her up and soothe her? What was wrong with me?

Without a doubt, I loved her. But I couldn't bring myself to love me as her mum.

I could love her like a mother – I just couldn't feel like one.

I had expected so much more from myself.

Frankly, my expectations of the whole bloody ordeal had been shattered.

I'd expected that, after the birth, I would get my body back.

I'd expected that, once she was here, we would finally be accepted by everyone and that all would be forgiven.

I'd expected that I would be a good mother; that I would know intrinsically how to care for my baby.

But I didn't.

When I'd prayed to God to let me keep my baby, and when I'd promised God that I would love, cherish, and care for my baby, I'd thought I could do it. Now I felt like I'd let myself – and everyone else, including God and my baby – down.

Bizarrely, despite feeling so desperately inadequate, I refused to let anyone help me.

Accepting assistance felt like admitting defeat. I also irrationally feared that the only reason why people were offering to help was because they were convinced that I couldn't do it and that I was useless.

In my intricately twisted psyche, any help offered reaffirmed my inadequacy.

I felt like shouting, 'Why are you always on my case? Can't you see I am trying to figure this fucking thing out?'

Or at least, 'Can you just leave me alone, so I can cry with my baby?'

I felt, in equal parts, desperate for the relief that would come with help, and desperate to prove that I could do it without help, because damnit, I should have been able to!

I wanted to be enough for her.

I had to be enough for her.

In the recesses of my mind, there was still hope that life would continue.

Life together with my new little sidekick.

My sidekick who, by now, had learnt to smile (and chose the moments where I was crying my heart out – usually for no apparent reason – to give me the cutest grins which exposed her toothless gums and lit up her whole chubby face).

I thanked heaven for those toothless gums, especially since the rest of my life was looming on the other side of this mum hustle.

When my daughter was three months old, I went back to school.

My mum had to fight with those who do not shrink from purpose to allow me to return. I needed to finish matric if I wanted to study further or to apply for a job.

The headmistress was not thrilled to have me back. She was adamant that I would do better if I skipped the rest of my matric year and reapplied again the following year. This obviously had nothing to do with the school's fear that I would tarnish their unblemished pass-rate record.

Grudgingly, I was allowed to return to school, and even though I had missed my matric trial exams, I wrote finals along with the rest of my class.

I passed.

In fact, I passed decidedly well.

I still harbour some serious resentment that no one (besides the headmistress) ever asked or gave a flying fuck about my grades in matric.

*cough* 'All A's.'

I mean, *cough* 'Mostly A's.'

Afrikaans was the subject that brought down my overall score.

Apparently, it was not only my foreign body and emotions that I had to navigate; I struggled with foreign languages too.

Fok.

# Pubs and Grubs

It happened one evening that I found myself at a squalid drinking establishment.

('Found myself' is not to be confused with actually taking myself there of my own volition. I was brought there by a friend who was having a hard time after a breakup.)

The owners of this establishment would probably refer to the place as a bar, or perhaps a pub.

I thought of it more as a watering hole.

A grubby watering hole.

The kind you might find at the Kruger National Park, with frenzied animals and hungry, archaic reptiles all over the joint.

We drank whatever was wet and vaguely potable.

Very classy.

While the drinking made my friend morose, it made me feel laid back and cheerful. Surprisingly, I didn't even feel bogged down by her gloomy mood.

Around 11 pm, my friend decided that she wanted to leave, and since I'd been taught that it was unbecoming for a single lady to be alone at a watering hole, I grabbed my handbag and followed her out.

We tottered down the two flights of stairs, grasping the sticky banister for dear life as our high heels threatened to cut and run from underneath us. I gave my weeping friend a hug and saw her into her Uber, while waiting for mine to arrive.

But as she pulled away, I made an uncalculated, spur-of-the-moment decision. I cancelled my ride.

I tottered back up the stairs, grasping the banister even more desperately and cursing the significant amount of money I'd spent on those wretched heels. How the sincerest fuck had my daughter walked all night in these things at her Met Gala?

They were super cute, though.

Back in the bar, I looked around.

At the pool tables to the left prowled several youngsters. Well, they were probably legally old enough to consume those appalling bright blue cocktails served in fish bowls with names like 'Coitus on the Coast' or something equally asinine. But they were still young enough for the males to take the severity of their hair spikes and the width of their stretched earlobes very seriously. The females, on the other hand, didn't appear to take anything seriously. They seemed quite content playing the bimbo/damsel card, giggling as they stretched over the pool tables with their pert little peaches peeping out of their spaghetti-strap tops. Meanwhile, the lithe, hubbly-smoking, skater-shoe-wearing boys draped themselves over them, ostensibly demonstrating to them how to use a pool cue.

To the right, slouched on the barstools, were a clutch of white-haired men, too drunk to hold themselves fully upright, leaning over tumblers of amber liquid they'd no doubt been nursing since lunchtime.

My gaze moved over to the dance floor (at least, I think that is what one would call the few square metres of vacant, beer-speckled floorspace between the bar and the toilets, where people gather to cavort and lip-sync to old music?)

I saw a group of middle-aged men and women, all just over-the-hill enough to not give a damn about what people thought of their cavorting and lip-syncing.

I wondered how to categorise this group. What would be the collective noun for a gathering of people of a certain age sending it to Michael Jackson's 'Beat It'?

A circle of seniors, perhaps?

A colony of cringers?

A rebound of ravers?

A moonwalk of mid-lifers?

A wobble of wannabes?

A vestige of vibers?

After spending much time observing them from the outside, I wondered what it would be like to be a part of that happy-go-lucky group of free-spirited, ageing misfits – just for one song.

I teetered up to the group and gingerly asked them if I could join them. Well, I guess shouting to be heard above the music could hardly be considered 'gingerly'.

A shortish man with fat rolls under his chin, chest, and belly mistook this volubility for self-confidence, and promptly swept me up into the circle. He had sweaty hands and unmemorable features.

Maybe they would have been more memorable had I been more sober.

We danced for over two hours.

(I abandoned the high heels after two minutes.)

I didn't have to do the stupid sexy-hands-in-the-air thing, because I was too busy clinging to him in fear that I'd be hurled into the surrounding patrons and set off a massive bar brawl.

I didn't touch any underboob, either (mine or his).

I didn't even step and tap.

For the first time in years, I didn't care how the evening would turn out. I didn't give a dog's bum-whisker about what people thought of me. I could not have given a tinker's damn if I would be going home with anyone, let alone worry about whether they might be the love of my life.

I was simply content with dancing (or trying to, at least). Albeit with a stranger whom I had no anticipation of ever meeting again.

Albeit in a place that probably only fronted as a bar, but was really an underground (although two storeys aboveground) gig for drug-hustlers. I was pretty sure the meth kitchen was tucked behind the loos.

It was a night to remember.

Pity I could barely remember it.

I can hardly even recall getting home that night.

The next morning, after a terrible night's sleep because the room refused to sit still, I woke up with a monstrous headache and heartburn that would make a dragon cry.

I surely couldn't feel so ghastly after just a few drinks.

My drink must have been spiked.

My eighteenth drink, most definitely, must have been spiked.

In spite of the physical discomfort, strangely, I felt happy.

My poor liver was clinging desperately to life – and I felt happy?

Well, alrighty, then.

I wouldn't have thought this would be the result of patronising dubious drug dens masquerading as drinking establishments. And I certainly did not plan to make a habit of doing this (mostly because my liver would give up and fall out of my bottom).

But despite going home without meeting the love of my life, I was surprisingly still content. I suddenly felt as though it didn't matter where I was, or who I was with, or what I was doing.

I just felt good.

Ironically, healing doesn't always come from great effort. In my case, I didn't achieve it through sacrifice or self-help books, or striving to stitch myself back together.

It didn't come from digging through the trenches of trauma, nor from mastering the elusive art of forgiveness.

It came when I stopped trying so hard to fix myself. When I finally let myself be free.

(Or as free as one can be while still wearing a bra.)

It came from dancing.

I needed to hold on – tightly, foolishly, joyfully – and then completely let go.

Somewhere between clutching a stranger and almost spraining my ankle, I found peace.

Go figure.

It only took some time – and a squalid drinking establishment – to reveal the truth: that healing doesn't always have to be a big, soapbox moment. Sometimes it shows up as a casual shrug. A laugh. A step to the left. A twirl. A finger-click. A moment you barely notice, because you're finally present within it. (And six tequila shots down.)

Contentment doesn't depend on romantic partners, professional success, or what you see in the mirror. It certainly doesn't comply with a plan. It's about appreciating the moment you're in, rather than constantly striving for a future outcome.

Time's quiet mercy softens blows, fades bruises, and grants us the absurd, astonishing permission to live without a plan.

In the end, I finally understood that the only requirement for living was to live.

Just as I was.

Gently frayed, not always sure of myself, and weirdly okay with it.

# Dates, Weights, and Cultured Debates

For the vast majority of my existence, my focus has been centred almost entirely on my weight, and the perception that others have of my ample exterior (especially my posterior). I mean, it would have been nice to be admired for my smarts, or my creativity, or my moral substance. But honestly? I just wanted to be pretty.

Okay, that's a lie.

I wanted to be beautiful.

Striking.

Gorgeous.

(Yes, there are subtle, but distinct, differences between these, and I wanted to be all of them.)

Unfortunately, my fixation with my weight and appearance overshadowed all the other things that made me who I was.

Alas, I've always had a tummy. A very conspicuous tummy. Very unlike an ironing board. In fact, I came out of utero with a tummy.

I just never lost my baby fat.

I mean, I totally have a six-pack, but no one can see it under all the layers of adipose tissue.

I do try to be articulate in my knowledge of human anatomy.

Very intelligent.

My body's cellular biology is very similar to that of marshmallows, with molecules comprising one part fluff, one part gelatine, and one part sarcasm.

Although I hadn't actually started dating yet, I mentally pencilled 'marshmallow density analyst' onto my hypothetical dating CV. At least it was more realistic than 'emotionally available'.

It didn't help that I compared myself to every other girl around me, including those in magazines, on TV, and on radio (I imagined that those sexy DJ voices were attached to very sexy bodies).

I was embarrassed that I was chunkier than everyone else. Even as a child, when I went to the beach with friends or on youth camps organised by the church, I always wore board shorts to hide my bottom and legs. Adolescence made this even worse, because by then I also had boobs which refused to stay inside those stupid polka-dot, triangle string-bikini tops that everyone was wearing, and that I, therefore, obviously had to wear too.

Like many young women, the opinions I had of myself were largely informed by what I thought others considered beautiful. I felt strongly that I should have a 'perfect' body and 'perfect' skin and 'perfect' hair, and I couldn't understand why – if I was supposed to have them – I didn't.

I was constantly reminded of my inadequacies, too.

When I was around seven, I was told that I would never be a dancer because I had chunky hands. Also, because I wasn't small enough. Or skinny enough. Or elegant enough. And my musical timing was very mediocre.

Way to piss on a little girl's dream, Mrs C Ruel.*

(*Not her real name, unfortunately.)

Look, perhaps Mrs C Ruel could have used a lesson in tact, but the fact did indeed remain that my hands were chunky and would be better suited to DJ gesticulation (whicky whicky), box packing, or airport marshalling. (You see why I can't be throwing my hands in the air and tantalisingly touching my body?)

Similarly, I was never likely to be a surfer.

I looked horrendous in a bikini. I still do (especially after breastfeeding, as my boobs now absolutely refuse to stay inside a bikini top, preferring to rest comfortably on my knees), but even before the rigours of motherhood worked their magic on my figure, I just never had the build for surfing.

Or the poise. I'm proficient in the very intricate arts of tripping up steps, and falling over flat surfaces, so any attempt to balance on a wheel-free skateboard in shark-infested waters while the ocean made me its bitch seemed plainly ridiculous. Even in board shorts.

What about running? I've already told you how much practice I've had at running, but when it comes to the actual sport, I suck.

Even if I enjoyed running – and I absolutely do not – my body is not made of the same substance as Usain's.

Yes, I'm on a first-name basis with him.

I sometimes call him Home Slice.

Sadly, he is not on a first-name basis with me.

He does not know my first, middle, or last names.

And he certainly wouldn't recognise me if I went up to him and called him Home Slice.

Anyhoo...

So, I accepted, I would never be a ballerina. The reason for this (and the same reason that I was unlikely to be the next Kelly Slater, and that I would not be competing alongside Home Slice) was because my body type didn't allow it.

That didn't mean that there was anything wrong with my body; it meant that my body was built differently.

Regrettably, all through my childhood (and well into my adulthood), I believed that I should be ashamed of my body. And the worst part is that – like so many girls and women out there – I chose to believe this. I chose to believe that I was no better than 'average'. I chose to believe that I didn't look good in a bikini, and that I shouldn't be seen wearing one in public.

So, despite living on the exquisite east coast of South Africa, with Blue Flag beaches for miles (literally), I avoided the beach in the same way that Lip Gloss Ladies avoid carbs.

And sugar.

And personality.

What was I talking about again?

Oh, Blue Flag beaches.

Full confession: my beach avoidance wasn't solely due to my embarrassment about my physique ('frame shame'? Could I be starting a hashtag?). It was also because the sun had a personal vendetta against me and my translucent, gecko-like skin. Also, because I didn't like having sand and sunblock invade my vagina or my inner ear canals. And because I could totally live without waves that managed to undress me faster than a 20-something horn dog.

But it was mostly due to fact that I hadn't developed a positive body image. I'd convinced myself that 'marshmallow' was not an acceptable body type, and that I needed to change my biological makeup. I'd decided that my body wasn't up to standard, and that it needed to be 'fixed' before I would be enough; before I could be loved.

Soapbox time!

Society constantly bombards us with images of the 'perfect' body, in the form of skinny as fuck (SAF) models and Lip Gloss Ladies who evidently spend an inordinate amount of money on Coffee and Cocaine.

In capital letters because they are treated as entire food groups.

Very important.

This onslaught of images of so-called 'perfection' has led many of us to the conclusion that our bodies are somehow inferior by comparison. And the 'aesthetic' industry has capitalised on this, inundating us with ways in which we can 'fix' (or at least improve) our appearance. Botox and dermal fillers, rhinoplasty and breast augmentation are no longer taboo. Plenty of high-profile 'TV personalities' now go for nose jobs, boob jobs, face jobs, butt jobs, and blow jobs – all while not really having actual jobs.

Side note: it is important to use verts liberally when talking about things that are accepted by society without any critical thinking, like 'TV personalities' and the 'aesthetic' industry. It shows that we are 'intellectually accomplished' and 'cultured'.

We're consistently exposed to a staple diet of dos and don'ts about how we should look and feel about ourselves. This propaganda is fed to us by cunning commercial culprits whose interest is NOT in helping us look and feel better. On the contrary – the worse we feel about ourselves, the more moolah they stand to make selling us their products, services, and promises. The bigger our bottoms, the bigger their bottom lines. It's the stuff that marketing dreams are made of.

However, perhaps the blame does not fall exclusively on the shoulders of advertising and social media.

Maybe some of it falls on us, and how we choose to discern information. Maybe we need to be more circumspect about the media, instead of simply devouring it like melktert and Smarties.

Seriously, if you haven't tried this combination, you should.

Not that I'm telling you what you should do.

The whole point of this rant is to liberate people from feeling like they should do anything.

But seriously, you should.

Okay, soapbox moment over.

The more I think about it, the more I realise how often this type of personal criticism and condemnation really is self-inflicted.

Do you know that I cannot remember a single time in my life when someone actually said I was fat?

Not a solitary soul ever told me that my legs were too thick. No one said I had preposterous boobs. No one pointed out that I had a superfluous chin. No one said my lips were too small or that Cupid had misplaced his bow. Not a single human ever complained that my tummy was yuck.

Sure, there might have been sniggers behind my back, but no one ever said to me, 'Gosh darn it, but you are all kinds of chunk.'

And even if they had, why the grandmother's cat should I have cared?

Do you know how fickle societal mores are? Not so long ago, people idolised Marilyn Monroe (whom I am convinced was – like me – made largely of pale, fluffy confectionary. The rest of her cellular makeup – unlike me – was sex appeal).

Ah, sex appeal.

I tried to do the sexy thing.

It just didn't work for me.

I started small. You know, with simple moves like making eyes with a guy while sucking seductively on a straw. The problem was that I usually missed the straw entirely, then had to embark on a series of giraffe-like tongue twists to try to recover it. (Can I take a moment to have a quick bitch about those shitty paper straws? I care, in theory, about the environment, but I don't think I should have to ingest the trees while I am enjoying my frappé. Sorry-not-sorry and thank you for listening.)

Another small move I tried was suggestively biting my lip.

Not the bottom one.

The top one.

I really can't understand why I am single.

Back to fickle social norms: after the Marilyn Monroe affinity wore off, the world became obsessed with ironing boards and SAF models who never smile. Probably because they are starving and miserable. Or because their face muscles have successfully atrophied after heaps of Botox.

Get that soapbox out again; I'm not done yet.

Tell me: if the world is so capricious, why do we choose to adopt narratives about our bodies that are likely to change the moment the next Kardashian has a brain fart?

It's all about conjured, faulty, and often contradictory opinions pulling a number on us. We see self-appointed health freaks indulging in avocado chocolate mousse, while there are doctors who suggest putting butter in our coffee (unless you want to be an SAF model). How the hamster's apron are we supposed to figure this out?

Why do we even have to figure it out at all?

Why don't we choose to take ownership of our self-appointed criticism? No one has the right to tell us that our bodies should be 'fixed' in order to be good enough, in order to get and keep a partner, or even in order to simply enjoy a trip to the beach (if getting sand and sunblock in your hoo-ha is your thing).

Healthy, positive self-image is grounded in self-worth, self-love, and owning our dignity. Of course, I didn't know that back then.

In my early 20s and dating (or attempting to), I was inexperienced, insulated, and deeply insecure – especially since my options seemed limited to men who mistook brandy breath for personality.

Desperate times; desperate measures.

I was propelled to attempt something I'd never tried before: a dating app.

A few desperate swipes at love, or something like that.

# Shit's Peak

With my house-building metaphor under control, my mind wandered to the next stage of healing. I knew it would be a tough journey, like a hike to a mountain summit, but I was up for it.

Geddit?

'Up' for it?

Too much?

Okay, moving right along...

That was my new metaphor: I was ready to summit my personal Everest.

But since I live in South Africa, I settled for the Drakensberg.

Slightly lower in altitude, but still metaphorically majestic.

Accordingly, I planned a getaway, on my own, to the Berg. This momentous (or mountainous) trip was going to be my chance to liberate myself from all the detritus that had been cluttering my life.

I needed to purge myself of all my crap – and laxatives would not help this type of purge.

Pity.

(I still packed them, 'just in case'.)

Around that time, I'd been thinking about an activity I'd participated in at a church youth group when I was younger. We wrote down our sins and hurts, folded the papers, and pinned them to a giant wooden cross – which was later set on fire (because nothing says 'cleansed and forgiven' like setting fire to shit). The exercise was meant to symbolise that whatever was holding us back was gone and we didn't need to carry it around anymore.

I decided I liked this imagery and I was going to replicate the scenario, but because I didn't have a huge cross lying around – and because unplanned burning of crap in my backyard was illegal – I planned to take all my shit, my baggage, and my hurts and leave it all at the mini holiday (or in the mini bar in my hotel suite).

I planned to return home totally refreshed, completely free of all my problems.

I was going to relax. I would wine and dine myself. Go on hikes.

Go horseback riding on mountain trails at sunset. I was going to bird watch (not). I was going to meditate in the wilderness and sit lakeside, observing the solitude. I was going to take long, candle-lit bubble baths and visit the spa.

I was going to come back a completely new person.

How marvellous?

It was all very exciting. This trip was going to mark the start date of me living again. After packing my bags (and my baggage), I set off for four days of alone time.

The initial drag on the busy, truck-burdened highway was a small price to pay for what was going to be such a magnificent trip. It wasn't long before the highway gave way to scenic, single-lane roads that traversed KZN, into the foothills of the Drakensberg.

There were goats grazing alongside the roads while their kids frolicked about nearby. There were herds of cows (and a few horses, who presumably identified as cows) in the roadside meadows.

There were cows in peoples' backyards. There were even rogue cows crossing the pothole-laden roads, guided by their rural herders who clearly didn't give two hoots about the prospect that vehicles could plough into their livestock at any given moment. I might have given more than two hoots. Loud ones.

Vast expanses of flowers adorned the hills in shades of yellow and pink. Tributaries of the Tugela River cut paths through the expanse of nature, hosting a colony of birdlife, members of which dipped in and out of the water as I glided past.

After a three-hour drive, I arrived at the resort and made my way to reception, where I proudly announced my arrival and checked into my suite.

My 'suite' was the size of a thimble in spandex, but it overlooked the resort, and this view, framed by the picturesque mountains in the distance, made up for the size.

I unpacked my clothes – and my baggage – ready to take on all the exciting things I had planned.

And then the heavens opened.

And, because it rained, I cried.

Once I started crying, I couldn't stop.

I didn't make it to the spa. I didn't hike. I didn't get to ride anything (equine or other). The only place I did go was round in excruciating circles of angry judgement and self-criticism.

I didn't meditate. I didn't go on quiet walks. I didn't have beautiful meals. I didn't have amazing experiences. I didn't have any grand revelations, and I was no closer to my big awakening than I was when I left home.

What I did do was gorge on room service and wallow in loneliness.

At first, I blamed the men in my life – mostly Tinder Guy and the Afrikaner – for my entire sorry situation. Obviously.

But then I started asking some deep, uncomfortable questions.

Real soul-searchers like: Why did this keep happening to me?

Why did I miss all the red flags? (Or, worse, see them and still stay?)

Although, on the surface, my relationships had looked so different, what did they have in common?

What was it about these men that drew me in, and then left me shattered?

And were they really to blame?

I mean, they both totally had the complete dick-head act down to a fine art, and caused me untold pain, and treated me badly, and lied through their unbelievably straight teeth.

Could they have spared my feelings? Sure.

Could they have been less manipulative? Less narcissistic?

Absolutely.

Could they have had some semblance of fucking decorum? 100%.

But maybe I could have owned some responsibility for my heartache as well.

At any point, I realised through my tears, I could have walked away. I chose to stay. They showed me that they were unable – or unwilling – to be faithful, and I chose to subject myself to this hurt.

To return to the home metaphor for a moment, I felt that if I lived in a glass house, throwing stones seemed risky business.

I was forced to confront not some other dipshit, but myself.

You know – that dipshit.

That made me cry even more.

I spent more time on that trip gazing at my own saggy navel than at the picturesque, stormy scenery outside my window.

Oh, did I mention that it rained?

The.

Entire.

Time.

I cried and slept and cried for four days. Zero enlightenment. Zero peace. Zero joy. Then I repacked all my things (and my baggage), and I drove it all the way home with me.

This time, I didn't notice the flowers or the streams or the goats or the cows. I drove without paying much attention to anything (except to avoid errant beasts entering the trajectory of my vehicle).

Which is just as well, since driving without paying attention is not very responsible, and I imagine that the rural herders would have had some serious beef with me if I were to take out any of their bovine.

I was so disappointed about the trip.

I'd genuinely thought that if I could just have gone somewhere inspirational to purge all my emotional baggage, that I would have come home feeling lighter and better.

Like if I'd gotten rid of everything, I would have been able to feel more comfortable in myself.

Suddenly, it hit me.

(Thank goodness, not a cow.)

I realised then that I'd been doing this my whole life: in one way or another, I'd repeatedly packed my unresolved junk into bags and carried it around with me, with the intention of dumping it at the next stop.

But each time, when I got to the next place, I'd pulled out the junk, arranged it prettily around myself like so much objets d'art, had whatever traumatising experience I needed to have there, then made damn sure to pack that shit right back up again before moving on to the next destination.

Maybe that was the problem. I saw 'home' as a destination I had to reach.

That, and my penchant for carting my crap along with me wherever I went.

My entire life, I'd been looking for a place where I belonged; a place to call home.

I'd tried my darndest to make a home for myself and my daughter, but I'd been building it on the foundations of turmoil. I'd been trying to build it on someone else's mess. I'd made a 'home' out of sheer steadfastness and resilience (balanced precariously on an imminent mudslide of sexual and emotional betrayal).

And until I came to terms with prospect that my idea of a 'home' was fucked, I would potentially never feel at home anywhere, and ultimately would continue to pack, repack and lug my baggage to every new destination, or person, or encounter.

I now mount my soapbox not with passion and zeal, but with a calm awareness and a knowing sigh.

I realise that the cliché 'home is where the heart is' was intended to imply that home is wherever love is. Wherever our loved ones are.

But what if we look at this completely differently?

Even literally?

Perhaps 'home' is where our heart is – right there inside of us.

Imagine if we didn't need anybody else to love us in order for us to feel at 'home'.

My grand realisation was that we needed to learn to love ourselves first. Home is a state of mind (or heart).

That was the reason why, even when I was surrounded by a loving family, I still felt out of place. It was the reason why, when I went through emotional turmoil, I was incapable of feeling at home anywhere, or with anyone.

Everywhere I went, my heart came with me. And because it didn't feel like home within me, nowhere I went was going to feel like home.

Home was where my heart was.

The epiphany was that I didn't need a suitable place to unpack the crap.

I didn't need to summit a mountain in order to reach the peak of my psychological progress.

I simply needed to take the entire bag off my shoulders and leave it wherever the hell I was at that moment.

I didn't need to unpack the pain. I didn't need to find the perfect place to leave it behind.

I just had to stop carrying it.

Maybe healing didn't have to be some perfect mountaintop moment. Maybe healing was finally realising that I'd been the one lugging the load all along – and deciding to set it down.

Right here.

Right now.

On the roadside.

In the rain.

With goat shit on my tyres and mascara down my cheeks.

But finally free.

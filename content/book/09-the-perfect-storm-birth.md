# Birth

The day before my baby was born, I had a three-hour drawing exam for which I had to go into school.

I can't even remember what I drew that day. Probably another outie navel. Or a large, green and pink fruit with tiny, insufferable seeds that completely ruin what should be a scrumptious summery refreshment.

I digress.

(I do that sometimes.)

I remember being escorted into an abandoned classroom, away from any other students. It smelled of mothballs and there were remnants of some scribble on the chalkboard. Remnants which would probably never be useful in actual life.

I remember my art teacher instructing me about the requirements of the exam in hushed tones, as though my very existence was an indictment on the education system, and she didn't want to draw attention to it.

She then left me alone in the closed room and was scheduled to return after the three hours had elapsed.

The silence was eery.

There were no sounds emanating from neighbouring classrooms. No screeching teachers or students engaging in some shitty group project. No gossip echoing down the corridors or cheers from a nearby sports field.

'Woohoo.'

'Go sports.'

'Etc.'

While drawing, I noticed that my baby hadn't moved for a while.

About ten minutes.

Then 20.

Then an hour.

Then two.

I got up on several occasions to bounce around and prod my belly. Not even star jumps (which caused sneaky farts here and there, so it was a good thing I was alone) helped to get the baby moving.

I couldn't shake the feeling that something was wrong.

Straight after that exam, I had an OB-GYN appointment (one of the last before my due date). The doctor told my mum(!) that the baby's heartrate was erratic and faint, which could signal foetal distress.

Perhaps the poor kid had simply grown tired of being continuously prodded, I thought. Maybe it was trying to say, 'Mum, I have only been able to sleep in ten-minute intervals for the past nine months. I need a break!'

Great, I thought. It's not even born yet, and I'm already upsetting it.

Crackers, I was so nervous that my farts turned into a subtle case of irritable bowel syndrome (IBS).

I was admitted to hospital and given an awful bacon and egg toastie, in the hope that stabilising my blood sugar would settle the baby's heartrate.

Still nothing.

It had been over six hours since I had felt any movement.

IBS turned into explosive diarrhoea.

The nurses hooked me up to machines to monitor the baby, and then left me while my mum went home to fetch my ready-packed hospital bag.

When my mum was in the room, the nurses (with their unholy hybrids of polyester and despair, and carrion-reeking breath) were attentive and explained the procedures to her in clear, soothing tones. When she left, they, too, disappeared.

They didn't speak to me. They didn't come when I pressed the call button to tell them that I was convinced I was having contractions. When the attending physician eventually stopped by and advised the nurses that I was in labour, they vanished as soon as the physician did.

They were probably off enjoying a rodent banquet at some racy nurse exhibit.

I was terrified.

Yes, I was alone, unnerved, and in pain – that was pretty terrifying enough. But I was more terrified that my baby had died.

Only once during that long, lonely night of terrible contractions and no sign of movement from my baby did a nurse come to check on me. When I said I was sore and worried, she gave me Gaviscon for indigestion, then promptly evaporated again.

By the next morning, I had had enough. I wanted to get the heck away from people who couldn't acknowledge me as a human being.

I just wanted my baby. I wanted to hold my child in my arms.

But things weren't moving along as they should have been.

After some discussion (between the doctor and my parents, obviously), I was prepped for an emergency C-section.

I was shaved (more like shorn, if you consider that I had only ever attempted to dehair my downtown once, and after weeks of scratching, decided against repeating it). I was dressed in hospital gowns, catheterised, and advised that someone would come to take me down to the operating theatre.

In all this time – since my admission to the hospital the day before – SD had not materialised. I had texted him, but he had not replied.

My folks had tried to call him, but he hadn't responded.

It was five minutes before I was wheeled into theatre when he made an appearance and was hastily prepped for the baby's arrival.

I'd been counting on my mum being there, because she'd been the one to attend all the prenatal classes with me. She'd held my legs back, rehearsing the birthing position. She'd counselled me after a class describing both natural and C-section births, when I'd raised my hand and asked the facilitator – altogether too seriously – whether there was a third option, because I was simply not prepared to choose either of those.

My mum was the one who was with me when I'd practiced how to breathe while holding a condom filled with ice. (This was supposed to teach me how to breathe through pain, because apparently frost-burnt fingers are equivalent to childbirth.)

Wasn't this the kind of shit that got me into trouble to start with? I'd mused.

Condoms are not for bananas or ice!

Is this a soapbox moment?

I suppose, since I was about to give birth, I didn't really have time to get on my soapbox, did I?

Okay, the soapbox would have to wait.

So, SD rushed in at the last minute and inserted himself into the procedure, and my mum – my safety net – was gone.

I imagine I should've been scared to undergo major abdominal surgery – but, honestly, I wasn't.

I think I'd become so programmed to expect trial and difficulty throughout my pregnancy that it didn't seem like such an enormous feat to have huge needles shoved in my back, or to be sliced open with a scalpel. Fear had long since become a companion. A friend who simply never left. (At least one friend that had stuck around.)

As it happened, the baby was wedged under my rib cage, so the anaesthetist pushed from the top end while, at the bottom end, the OB-GYN gave a running commentary to the not-so-sexy nursing staff about navigating the cord around the neck. Or at least that is what I thought was happening, judging by the medical jargon being bandied about.

A little while later, the OB-GYN turned briefly to SD, who was standing at my shoulder. 'It's a girl,' she announced, before continuing with whatever she was doing to my internal organs.

A girl, I thought. How wonderful.

My perfect daughter arrived in the afternoon of 29 May 2008.

A precious gift from God, I mused.

Then I noticed the silence.

I didn't hear anything other than the occasional beeping of medical machinery.

'What's going on?' I croaked.

Silence.

My thoughts became frantic. I pictured every possible worst outcome.

I was convinced that my daughter was stillborn.

Ah fuck, this is because I backchatted my folks, isn't it? I thought.

It's because I didn't live up to my promise about not asking for anything, I fretted.

I kept asking questions, and no one answered.

'Is she okay?'

Silence.

'Is she alive?'

Silence.

'Did you get the cord off from around her neck?'

Silence.

It was as though I simply wasn't there. As though I was merely an inanimate vessel, used to incubate a little human until she was able to live on her own in the world.

Was my little human going to live?

She still hadn't made a sound.

Out of the corner of my eye, I watched the nurses' backs as they huddled over my baby. They whispered to each other, as if comparing notes, and appeared to be taking turns to handle her. In the absence of any sound from my child, their whispers were overpowering. In my inexperienced opinion, they sounded frantic, and their rubbing and prodding seemed forceful. Why were they smacking my baby so hard? Why were they scrubbing her so vigorously? This honey had been protected in my tummy for nine months. Then she came headfirst into this freezing world only to be roughly handled by nurses who couldn't be sexy even if they splurged on fishnet stockings and endeavoured to brush their teeth.

Surely one shouldn't treat a newborn baby like that on the first day of her life?

Was she even alive?

No one had bothered to tell me that babies born by C-section often don't cry until the amniotic fluid is suctioned out of their noses and mouths.

Maybe they had told my mum.

Finally, I heard it: that little cat-like cry which signalled life.

I was relieved, but I wasn't overwhelmed with emotion.

I didn't cry.

Maybe I should have. Did everyone else cry when they saw their baby for the first time?

That was the only time in my life that I could bear the sound of crying. That little cry marked my baby's safe arrival. It was the first time that she breathed, and actually, the first time in months that I felt like I could breathe.

My little spatchcock chicken was sprawled across a table under a warming light, which cast a red hue over her. It was there where her APGAR tests were carried out and where she was cleaned up and wrapped in a warm blanket and handed over to SD.

SD.

He held my baby first.

The hospital staff spoke to him. They answered his questions.

They gave him my baby to bring to me.

One of the nurses snapped a photo of the three of us. In the pic, my daughter was swaddled tightly in a hospital blanket, her puffy face and oh-so-cute, pursed lips on display for the camera. But at the time, I could only see the back of her head.

SD held her in front of me while I lay helpless on the operating table. I was her mother, but no one had thought to ask if I'd like to hold my baby. She and I had been inseparable for nine months, and in the first nine minutes since she'd been born, everyone else in the room – including total strangers – had spent more time with her than I had. It was as if none of them thought I should – or could – be trusted with such a precious treasure. I had no say in the matter.

I felt like my autonomy had been ripped away along with my baby.

Partially paralysed and completely dazed, I watched this with a certain detachment. The simpering nurse. The bright flash of the camera. SD's leering face.

Looking back at that photograph, it represented for me a mockery of my role as a mother – something that, as we shall see, would have enduring repercussions.

It was the first picture ever taken that affirmed that I was her mum.

And it was shared with someone who wasn't even sure if he wanted to be a dad.

It hit me like a sucker punch: I had to share my baby with him.

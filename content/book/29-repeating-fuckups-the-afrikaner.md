# The Afrikaner

Life carried on in its obstinate way.

It didn't take a breather. It didn't cut me slack or give me a moment for introspection.

Life didn't feel sorry for me.

Pity.

I got up every day and slapped a bright smile on my face.

I went to work, attended meetings, and started studying towards another diploma. I paid bills and made dinners.

I began making my way up the corporate ladder (which is the only ladder I am prepared to climb, given that I possess a genuine fear of heights, and a general disdain for physical exertion). I was promoted to the very prestigious, ultra-important, super-adventurous, not-boring-at-all position of Head of Claims at the insurance firm where I worked.

It's a party out there.

At least it took a little pressure off my financial situation (as in: I no longer researched how to sell my organs on the black market).

I concentrated on building my relationship with my kid and showing her that I was there for her. I showed interest in her projects and activities. I knew you couldn't make up for lost time, but we sure were giving it our best go.

Besides for the small problem that I had to get fully dressed before walking from the shower back to my bedroom (where the clothes were), or the fact that I couldn't simply turn down the TV when my folks watched it at volumes heard by the deaf, life was good.

Now that my emotional real estate was no longer taken up by a tenant who refused to pay rent, it was filling up with something that looked an awful lot like contentment.

Then, one day, a vehicle insurance claim landed on my very prestigious, ultra-important, super-adventurous, not-boring-at-all desk. Followed shortly by another one concerning the same fleet.

The owner of this fleet was one of our firm's biggest national clients.

After a few more incidents involving this fleet and its very clumsy drivers, I had cause to communicate with the proprietor of the company. The bulk of our dealings were over email, but there was the odd occasion when we spoke telephonically.

He had an Afrikaans name, which – for an English-speaking human – was rather difficult to pronounce. Ahead of a phone call, I would take myself off to the bathroom to practice saying his name so that – when I was patched through to the switchboard of his company – I had no problems requesting to speak to him.

Thankfully, I don't think that anyone heard me breathily repeating his name in a public toilet cubicle.

Along with the Afrikaans name came a fierce Afrikaans accent.

Ordinarily, I would have found this off-putting. Stupid really, but those with English accents sometimes consider themselves superior.

It's me.

I'm those.

It was different with him, though. His accent made him sound cultured. Articulate. Wise. More than his accent, his tone of voice was irresistible. It was warm and smooth, and if a voice could sound safe, his did. I lost time thinking about that voice. After a phone call with him – which was purely professional – I would be left feeling flushed and all tingly and stuff.

In one of the more tenuous claims where a vehicle was badly damaged under questionable circumstances, we had more dealings with each other than usual. Despite the fact that – I imagine – this was somewhat beneath his pay grade (given his fancy 'business owner' title), he chose to deal with me himself.

Perhaps he ordered his underlings to stand down so that he could communicate with me directly.

Wishful thinking, much?

Some of the discussions around the claim settlement took place at odd hours, like during lunchbreaks, or after he got home from the office in the evenings. Rather than using work lines, our communication moved to our private phones. Emails gave way to text messages.

Gradually, our discussions became more friendly.

Even flirty.

I'm not good at flirting.

He said, 'I like your profile picture – you're really pretty.'

I said, 'So are you.'

(His profile picture was a tree.)

Despite my dismal attempts at coquetry, we continued to chat. I discovered that he was ten years older than me, a chartered accountant by trade, that he enjoyed golf and fishing, and – because he eventually sent me a picture of himself – that he was really hot.

Like, stupidly good-looking.

Like, go-and-change-your-knickers good-looking.

Before I knew it, we were messaging daily. He took a real interest in all aspects of my life. He enquired after my daughter and asked thoughtful questions about my work. He paid attention to what I said, and would follow up on topics and stories I'd brought up. He even called me every week just to chat.

Like, actually telephonically.

I'm not sure if all cell phones are even able to make and receive calls anymore. It seems that most guys' phones only come with WhatsApp, Instagram, Facebook, Snapchat, and Tik Tok these days.

Although we flirted here and there, our relationship had its foundations built firmly on friendship. He was an excellent friend, and I was always excited to talk to him. I enjoyed hearing his opinion on things and felt as though I could have my own opinion, and it was taken seriously.

He never patronised me. When I told him how I felt about something, or gave my take on a topic, he was genuinely interested in my thoughts. It never felt like he was just listening to me to humour me or appease me.

I confided in him about my disastrous relationship with Tinder Guy. I told him about all the highs and lows, and all the hurts. He reciprocated, giving me insight into the good and bad of his previous relationships.

He wasn't judgemental. He listened intently. He asked intelligent questions, and he offered me support.

He was always ready to offer help. I once sent him a voice note in which my obnoxious windscreen wipers had drowned out my message. The next day, he insisted on replacing the wipers for me.

Short soapbox moment:

Women are genetically hardwired to detest the idea of spending our own money on anything car-related. We would rather use our salary for online shopping, chemical peels, and manicures, thank you very much. Neither petrol nor car maintenance of any kind is worthy of our hard-earned cash.

I have totally found myself on the side of the road without fuel on more than one occasion.

Not a damsel, though.

A little distressed, I'll give you that.

Of course, I declined his offer because I was a strong, independent, successful, wise, intelligent woman on the cusp of learning how to survive 30 and life in general, and on the brink of purpose.

Incepto ne Desistam.

Also, don't give in to humans who want to buy you windscreen wipers.

They will think you are DTF.

Right, back to the Afrikaner. We chatted about trivial things, things of little consequence. We also chatted about bigger things: our hopes and dreams.

Uncannily, these were very similar. We each dreamed of a plot of land with rolling hills and a big family home with a wraparound porch. We each envisioned our family housed at the far corner of the property – far enough not to interfere, but close enough that when they hit their twilight years, we could, if need be, ensure that they didn't burn their house down or wander into Pick n Pay in their underpants.

Eventually, we combined the dreams, with his family in one corner of the estate, and mine in the other.

He said that he wanted two children, plus mine, which made three.

Surprisingly, after that comment, I could still hear him talking about our future plans, even over the loud humming of my ovaries.

Our relationship became more involved over time, and the more I got to know him, the more he became important to me. He became more important than my hurt or than my past. I began to think I could really give things another chance with a guy like this.

God, was I really doing this again?

Was I honestly back to picturing porches and future kids and HEAs – after everything?

Maybe I was. And maybe that terrified me more than it thrilled me.

But I felt myself softening. Trusting. Beginning to hope.

And one day, whilst fantasising about where we might actually build this dream life, the locations he kept suggesting gave something away:

He lived in Cape Town.

Two thousand kilometres away.

My ovaries cried betrayal.

So, you know, if you can't get someone to commit to you locally, try to find someone at the other end of the country.

# Living Large

I was a big girl now.

And not just because of the pizza, burgers, and ice cream.

Ooh, and milkshakes. Did I mention I'd discovered milkshakes?

The double-thick ones. The ones that are difficult to suck through a straw. The ones you have to eat with a spoon. The ones drizzled in chocolate sauce.

Fine, I had basically rediscovered ice cream. But it tasted different in a tall glass!

Anyway, I had managed to locate my big-girl panties.

I'd squeezed them over my podgy cooch, and I'd gotten the spring back in my step. You know the one – that half-squat, half-shimmy bounce when you're trying to free your broekies from your butt-crack.

I no longer got ill all the time. I no longer felt crazy and unhinged.

Gone were the long evenings of crying and the even longer days when I couldn't wait to crawl into the comfort of my own bed and fall apart.

The foundations of my internal 'home' were more stable, and things at the house where I actually lived became easier, too. I found my groove with the 'roommates'. We settled into a nice, easy existence. We took turns making dinner, and ate together. We split the bills. We all pitched in with cleaning the house (except for my daughter, who always seemed to have a ton of homework when the floors needed mopping) and we each did our bit to help with chores, like the laundry (as in: I always ensured that my dirty laundry was in the wash basket so the laundry fairy could miraculously wash, dry, and iron it).

We did the collective parenting thing pretty well, too. We all helped my daughter with her homework and the plethora of projects she came home with. Sometimes she would tell us politely that she didn't need our help. Sometimes she wasn't so polite.

If I was stuck in a very prestigious, ultra-important, super-adventurous, not-boring-at-all meeting and unable to collect my kid from school, my parents would fetch her. They took this opportunity to elevate themselves – in my kid's mind – to VIPs, because they bought her ice creams and treats and didn't 'ask boring questions' on the drive home.

Personally, I felt it necessary to establish how my kid's day was, whether she had any homework, the extent to which she needed assistance with said homework, and whether there were any new developments in her friendship circles.

My kid didn't appreciate this. She felt that she should be able to communicate in grunts and vague gesticulations, rather than engage in any meaningful conversation straight after school.

So help me, I became even more passionate about her development than I'd been before. I was fascinated by everything she was involved in. I was attentive to her thoughts on things like sexual identity (since this was super topical in her #woke generation). I was supportive of the plans she made with her friends (even if I was only informed about these plans five minutes before she needed me to transport her to them). Honestly, I didn't mind being reduced to a taxi service – I picked up more intel eavesdropping on teen gossip than I ever could through direct questioning.

It was a joy to watch my daughter grow up. She had moments where she displayed all the typical attributes of a wise and confident teen. In fact, I began referring to her as Oh-Wise-One, because she seemed to know more about everything than anyone around her (though she was very patient in deigning to put up with us intellectual duds).

She could outfox anyone. For instance, I once reprimanded her for whining.

'For fuck's sake,' I said. 'Can you please just talk to me without whining?'

Her response? Calm as fuck, the kid looked me in the eye and said, 'And can you please stop swearing at me?'

Naturally, I had to educate her on the nuanced difference between swearing at her and swearing at the situation – something her developing teenage brain couldn't yet possibly understand.

At which point she took the opportunity to educate me that she was whining at the situation, and not at me.

I was so impressed by her witty one-upmanship.

Not.

Luckily, these moments were few and far between.

She was becoming a young lady, in that she had reached the stage of puberty that all little girls dread: boobs and blood. It was usually around a particular time of the month that she did the iffy-teen thing. But for the most part, she was a flipping good kid.

She was kind. She cared fervently for her friends. She never got into trouble at school. If she was rude (or did the grunting thing in response to my questions) she always apologised later. She studied without being reminded. She went to bed without being begged.

She didn't swear (even though her mum could out-cuss a sailor). She was not a rulebreaker or rebel.

She was a 'full-arser'. She gave her utmost in absolutely everything she did. She was eager to try everything, whether it was playing the drums, checking out a new dance class, having a shot at playing goalie on a soccer team, or taking up another subject. She never half-arsed a thing.

She had a passion for life that – given her difficult start, and a mum who didn't always have her shit together – was almost miraculous.

She carried herself so responsibly and gracefully. I was insanely proud of her.

I was also proud of myself. I had a sense of purpose and found solace in living with my folks and their guidance and support.

I'd spent an inordinate amount of my adult life wanting a family.

Yet, when I took stock of my life, I realised that I'd had one all along.

Sure, it would have been nice to be able to walk from the shower to my own room in the buff. I sometimes wished that, when I didn't feel like cooking meat and three veg, I could feed my kid microwave popcorn for dinner.

Or McDonald's.

Not having these things was a small price to pay for my sanity, though. And they were modest sacrifices to see my kid thriving.

I still sometimes wished I had the privacy of a place where I didn't run the risk of getting caught in the act of crying, or whatever else I wanted to do on my own.

Like mum things.

Like single mum things.

Tinder Guy was a distant memory. I no longer needed to switch radio stations if a song came on that reminded me of him. I didn't feel queasy if I ate something that reminded me of our time together, especially charcuterie boards. (This was a good thing, because charcuterie had become a firm favourite of mine.) I no longer felt insane amounts of regret for having been so ridiculously stupid and perpetuating a relationship with him for so long. I no longer even referred to it as a situationship.

I still thought of the Afrikaner sometimes, but the reflections had changed and matured. I stopped praying that he'd eventually want to come back to me. I stopped hoping that he would realise he'd made a mistake. I no longer dreamed about him moving to my hometown, or me moving to his. The thoughts – while still dusted with a light coating of rejection – transformed from intense longing to cool acceptance.

I was proud of that progress.

I'll admit there were a few regrettable late-night essays sent to the fuckwit, explaining – at length – why he was human trash. It still bothered me that he'd ended things without even giving me a say. So, in my mind, I stormed into his fancy-ass office, slammed down my soapbox, climbed up (hoping it would hold my added weight), and let rip at him.

About the lies.

The emotional cowardice.

The romantic recklessness.

But that was my ego talking. In my heart, I knew I was better off without him.

He had rejected me – and that told me everything I needed to know. It also meant that I would never wonder if I'd made a mistake.

For me, it was final.

For him, though, it might never be.

He would have to carry that burden.

I left all the emotional baggage behind – for him to unpack. I wasn't carrying that shit anymore.

'Suck on that, arsehole.'

See what emotional progress I'd made?

I'd taken large strides in my psychological development.

In fact, 'large' became the new buzzword for my life.

Large panties.

Large boobs.

Large milkshakes.

Large feelings I didn't always understand.

Large lessons learnt the hard way.

Large personal growth.

I was large – and finally, gloriously – in charge of my life.

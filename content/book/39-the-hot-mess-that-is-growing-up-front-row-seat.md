# Front-Row Seat

We all grow up eventually. It just takes some of us longer than others.

While I was working through my issues with boys (not to be confused with men), my daughter – being at an all-girls school – had few opportunities to meet any boys herself.

Like me at that age, she was rather awkward around the opposite sex.

This, of course, was my opinion.

She'd no doubt have disagreed. Mostly because 14 is the age when kids purposely disagree with their parents. About everything. Even if they actually do agree with their parents, they still express disapproval of some kind. To cope with this, I usually resorted to giggling inwardly, and agreeing with her simply to avoid conflict.

'Sure, Oh-Wise-One, your friend should not have gotten into trouble for submitting her essay late.'

'Sure, feta is not a cheese.'

'Sure, people should be legally permitted to identify as cats.'

'Sure, girls should be allowed to have far more social interaction with the opposite sex (even when "the opposite sex" gets to decide whether their sex is, in fact, opposite or not).'

And, because her school evidently agreed with her, they arranged an event with a neighbouring boys' school.

A social.

Leading up to this grand event, homework was thrown to the wind. So was studying. Even the prospect of going to school (rather than spending the day planning outfits) was, according to Oh-Wise-One, expecting far too much.

My daughter and her friends chattered incessantly about what they would wear, whether they would put on makeup, and how they would style their hair. Were they aware that this was a Grade 8 social, not the Met Gala?

For context: my kid and her friends were the type who usually wore Star Wars T-shirts and baggy jeans with Vans sneakers, scuffed at the toes. They marched to the beat of their own drums. They were drama students, gamers, singers, and musicians. They weren't particularly friendly with the popular Lip Gloss Ladies crowd and didn't appear to give a whoop about it, either. It was hard to imagine any of them in dresses with their eyebrows on fleek.

When she was tiny, I used to dress my daughter in darling little frocks. Well, at least until she was around three years old. After that, she dressed herself and had a very keen idea of exactly what she would and wouldn't wear. She usually went for a layered ensemble, comprising an adorable tulle skirt over a pair of shorts over a pair of leggings. On top, she would pick out a T-shirt over a dress, with a jacket to (not) match.

By the time she hit adolescence, though, there was not a dress to be seen in her wardrobe.

Dresses were – according to Oh-Wise-One – ultra-uncool.

Until the social.

Then she wanted a deep-red, knee-length dress with lace accents.

It was to be fitted around the bust – you don't call them boobs when you are talking about sewing, I was advised sternly – and would flare out at the waist.

Basically, the kind of dress that would make a little girl want to spin in circles and watch as it swirled around her. (At 14, apparently, you had to do the swirling in the privacy of your own room so that other people couldn't see that you were still – in some respects – an excited little girl).

I made the dress for her. I sewed for hours every night for a week – and without any help from singing rodents like in Disney films.

Nope, I got home from work, wolfed down dinner, then slouched over the sewing machine for hours while my daughter caught up on TikTok.

But it was ready on time, and she was ecstatic about it.

(Not that she would let me see her in it. She'd become so secretive all of a sudden. Something to do with teenagers needing to develop their own identity and sense of independence or some such. I couldn't understand why she no longer confided in me about the fart she had on board, or proudly came to show me her snot.

Apparently, it was no longer considered acceptable to give me detailed accounts of her bodily functions. Can't understand why.)

On the day of the social, I picked her up from school. I'd taken the afternoon off from work because I wanted to be available for this special 'first'. I was so looking forward to spending time with her and gabbing about girly things while helping her with her makeup.

'No, your face is not a colouring-in book,' I planned to say.

'If we move your fringe off your face, and fasten it with a pretty clip, we'll be able to see how gorgeous your big, blue eyes are.'

It turned out that she had other things in mind for getting ready.

A closed door was one of them.

With her on one side of it, and me on the other.

Ouch.

Not exactly what I'd expected.

I sat outside her room like a drunk stalker, praying that she might have a change of heart and let me in so I could share this momentous occasion with her.

Extraordinary that I could make her first social all about me.

Epic mothering right there.

When she finally came out of her room, I had to catch myself before I hit my head on the floorboards (I'd dozed off leaning on her door). And then I had to catch my breath.

She barely resembled my little girl.

She looked like a woman.

She was gorgeous.

Her makeup had been applied with a deft hand, as though she'd been doing it all her life. She had strategically put on only a touch of foundation (to cover those mischievous teenage zits), a bit of blush, mascara, and some expertly positioned highlighter on her cheek bones.

No Lip Gloss in sight.

She was stunning.

A swan.

Not that she'd ever been an ugly duckling.

For all her grownup demeanour, she seemed a little uneasy. Like a child trying to balance in her mother's red, patent leather high heels (which was, incidentally, precisely the case here.)

I saw a flicker of myself at that age – self-conscious, hopeful, and entirely unprepared for what boys could be.

The drive to the social was an oddly quiet one. We were both palpably nervous. Her nerves were the kind that made your tummy flutter with anticipation; mine were the kind that made your tummy gurgle and resulted in the need to fart repeatedly.

Mercs, Land Rovers and Porsches clogged the entrance to the school. The obnoxious fuckers didn't even deign to park properly, straddling bays so there was nowhere to park even my tiny VW.

As it turned out, I didn't need to park, because my kid mumbled goodbye and performed what I can only describe as a teenage tuck-and-roll to get out of my (still moving) car as soon as she spotted her friends – also barely recognisable in dresses and makeup.

It didn't seem real. It was like watching a play, with all of them in full costume.

Only, I didn't have a ticket to the show.

It was opening night, and I had to wait in the lobby while the action played out onstage in the theatre.

I suddenly felt teary and anxious.

It wasn't about being left out of the getting-ready process.

It wasn't about the dismissive tuck-and-roll goodbye.

It was because, without warning, I found myself right back there on that hospital bed, at 17, holding my perfect baby, whom I felt I didn't deserve and whom I didn't want to share with anyone.

She was too precious to be let out of my sight.

She was too precious to be exposed to boys.

It was too hard to watch my little baby grow up.

Given my own experiences with boys at high school, I felt nervous for her. I was worried that a boy would say something nasty to her or make her feel bad about herself or, worse, lump her in the Friend Zone for all eternity.

Not that I was projecting or anything.

Speaking of eternity, that's what it felt like waiting until the 10 pm pickup time.

I was dying to know how the evening went, but I'd resigned myself to the fact that the most I'd get was probably a mumble.

When she got in the car, though, she had so much to tell me that it all came pouring out like a burst dam wall.

'We had burgers for dinner, Mum. And it was so weird because the boys stood on the sides of the hall and didn't really talk to any of us. But we also didn't really talk to them. And then we had to dance with them, because the teachers told us to. And my friends and I didn't really know how to dance with them, so we kind of just all stood in a circle and, like, looked at each other. It was totally awkward. And then right at the end, Mum, there were some boys who came to talk to us. And I don't really remember what we talked about, but there was this boy who I liked. He had dark, curly hair and a tan. I really wanted him to ask me for my number, but he didn't. So, I told my friend that she must ask him to ask me. And then my friend said I must just go and ask him for his. So, I did. I walked straight up to him, and I got his number. But now I don't know if I should message him. Will he message me, Mum? Oh, no, wait – I didn't give him my number! How could he message me? Oh my gosh, why didn't I give him my number? What will I say to him? Do you think I should wait until tomorrow to message him? Maybe I should just message him now. Yes, I think I'll just take the plunge and message him now. I hope he messages me back! But I don't want things to go too fast. Just because we're texting doesn't mean we're dating. I think, Mum, it would be better if we were just friends for a while, until I get to know him. Then when I know him well enough, maybe we could date. But I totally think all of us girls should just be friends with these guys. I mean, we don't even really know them, right? Okay, I am going to message him and tell him that I am keen to get to know him. What do you think, Mum?'

She didn't take a single breath during her lengthy soliloquy, which lasted the whole car ride home.

No mumbling.

No unintelligible gesticulations.

I definitely hadn't expected that!

I'd just spent hours prepping myself to accept that she would probably offer me a salutatory grunt and then beg me to stop 'nagging' her for details. I'd figured I'd have to pry them out of her with a wrench at a later stage.

I also hadn't expected her pluck. That was one audacious babe, I thought! I was so impressed that she'd had the guts not only to ask a boy she'd barely met for his number, but to follow through and make the first move to communicate. All while being level-headed enough not to rush into things.

Wise.

I got all teary again, but this time they were tears of joy and pride, because she was just so jolly clever and beautiful and flipping incredible. I was so proud of my baby girl, even though she was clearly no longer my baby girl.

On reflection, I mused, her flirting prowess might need a bit of work. If she was going to turn out anything like her mother, though, she might never acquire these skills.

Sigh.

I'd at least have to warn her to avoid drinking straws. And lip biting.

Would she even want my advice?

Maybe.

Those words still rung through my head like music: 'What do you think, Mum?'

She still wanted me in her life, even if it was only to smile encouragingly at her from the wings.

What a privilege it was to watch her shine as she made her debut performance into adulthood.

I realised that, even if she occasionally closed the door, I had a front-row seat to her life, and every night was opening night.

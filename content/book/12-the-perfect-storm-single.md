# Single

It only took five months of life with a baby before SD and I decided that we were not going to work. It came as a kind of relief. I was so tired of carrying around a dead weight along with the weight of my decisions (not to mention several kilograms of baby and all the pregnancy kilos I hadn't lost).

It was difficult to be in a relationship with someone who was not responsible, who refused to stick to any kind of routine, who was never available when he was needed, and never really present when he was there. I thanked God for my parents, who sacrificed everything to support me and my daughter.

When SD left, he was given the opportunity to see his daughter whenever he wanted – though that didn't last long. He soon began dating someone else, and was keen to bring this 'her' to visit my kid along with him. When I demurred, he insisted that he pick the baby up for visitation. (She was still being breastfed at the time.)

I was quite happy to get up onto my soapbox – leaky boobs and all – and tell him (and his floss) that 'I have no moral problem with a new significant other being in my child's life. I'd just prefer it if you introduced her to my child once you were a sure thing. You know, like when you're satisfied she is not a psycho. Until then, I'm not prepared to hand my kid off to every new flavour of Lip Gloss that traipses in and out of your life. Moreover, I would really like it if the total number of introductions of partners was limited, so my kid won't have to potentially learn to love new people only to have them torn away from her again and again. I wouldn't want her to grow accustomed to the idea that partners are to be changed with the same frequency of underwear. Please and thank you.'

Hard to deliver a monologue on parental consistency and be taken seriously with milk streaming down your chest. It might have gone down better had I talked to him about this when not amount my metaphorical soapbox.

Nevertheless, as time went on, visitation became too inconvenient for him.

I didn't purposefully make it difficult for him. I never said he couldn't have a relationship with her, or that she may not see him. Financial support wasn't a proviso for visitation (though it would have been nice).

There were some logistical challenges. I felt strongly that my daughter should remain in a routine and a familiar environment so that she was always content and at peace; he wanted to take her at odd times, at his convenience.

Perhaps the best course of action would have been to meet at a neutral venue, but it was tricky to coordinate this because I still wasn't old enough to drive, so I couldn't simply meet him at a park or play zone without the assistance of my parents.

By this stage in her development, my daughter was on the brink of sitting. I would prop her up with cushions (to ensure that she didn't break her little back by toppling over) and she seemed to enjoy doing this precarious sit-and-lean-against-her-little-pot-belly thing, probably because it was easier for her than actually sitting up straight. She reached for anything she could get her chunky little hands on, and then – because she evidently had a penchant for death by way of choking – shoved it all into her mouth at the speed of lightning.

Then she started crawling, and she appeared to have been born with an internal navigation system that pointed her in the direction of all the electrical sockets.

Shocking, really.

(See what I did there?)

When she wasn't trying to kill herself by way of inhaled objects or electrocution, she enjoyed playing with anything that made lots of noise. Pots, pans, and wooden spoons made ideal instruments. They were also effective tools to jolt me straight out of whatever reverie had held me captive momentarily.

There were times when she was a little more niggly, like when she was teething, or when she became annoyed with her body's limitations. All she wanted was to be able to do things on her own. She seemed desperate to be independent, while I struggled to adapt to the independence and isolation that were thrust upon me when I became a single parent.

I certainly wasn't getting any support from SD. Look, it's not like he was some god-awful reprobate who'd throw a punch at anyone just to watch them fall. He just didn't care enough to be involved. I don't believe he ever made a conscious decision to hurt his child. I just don't think he made a conscious decision to be there for her, either.

He visited less and less frequently.

Once a week became once a month and then very soon once a year. In total there were only a handful of visits and, after that, a few phone calls.

Turns out, absence doesn't always announce itself with slamming doors. Sometimes, it creeps in without a sound. In this case, it slipped in when he slipped out.

He just stopped showing up.

He moved provinces, and then moved overseas.

And so, there I was, still in the poo, still feeling decidedly inadequate, and now I was on my own.

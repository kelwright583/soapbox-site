# Like a Winner

I was, for several years (it was two, but 'several' sounds more impressive), the Liaison Mum for my kid's class. This very prestigious, completely-above-average job entailed communicating with the other parents items of severe importance, like the date of the annual school fair.

Very important.

It's the winning thing to do.

Jokes.

Volunteering to be Liaison Mum (did I mention that this very prestigious job was pro bono?) is really just the thing to do to try to prove to oneself and others (mostly others) that you take your kid's schooling ultra seriously and that you are #wellputtogether and #capable.

Trying not to be average.

Trying not to be a benchwarmer.

This glamorous position had the added benefit of keeping one up to date with gossip (being the intermediary between the parents and the school) and making one feel oh-so-fabulous, building relationships with a newfound host of preposterously perfect mum friends (who would not recognise you if they ran into you on the immaculate school grounds. It turns out that text message communication isn't the same as a real friendship.)

I got heaps of exercise, as I was run off my feet sending out reminders for yet another donation appeal for yet another cause, yet another raffle, yet another cupcake day, exhibition, art project, or charitable collection. The astronomic private school fees were clearly insufficient, perhaps due to the maintenance of all those immaculate grounds.

I also had the privilege of managing monetary collections for the teacher's end-of-year present (as well as the presents for Easter and Valentine's Day and Mother's Day and Women's Day and Teacher's Appreciation Day and any other significant days, like Wednesday), for which, incidentally, I had to lay out the cash. It's great when the rich and famous 'forget' to repay you, and you, as a single mum, have to cut back on toothpaste at the end of the month because it's too embarrassing to remind the very clever, very stressed #stayathomemum with the big allowance that she owes you money.

Also, she is probably #toobusy getting her own exercise with gym (I mean Jim).

Deep breath.

#notangryjustdisappointed

When my daughter was nearing the end of senior primary, I received a message from another mum (of a child in my daughter's class) who was concerned because her daughter had witnessed an event in the school library which had caused her distress.

Feeling it wasn't my place to pry for details, I simply passed on the message to the form teacher.

What is with people who disclose only enough information to pique your interest, but then hold back the juicy bits, as if to assert their superiority by the notion that they have classified intel?

Give me the damn deets, peeps!

What happened in the library?

Why was your kid distressed?

Spill the beans, for goodness' sake!

Bunch of fruitcakes.

Obviously, I didn't say any of this to the fruitc... I mean, woman.

Being the very mature Liaison Mum that I was, I advised the teacher that she ought to use her discretion as to whether this was an issue which required further inquiry.

If you are a Liaison Mum, you must use words like 'ought to' and 'discretion'.

Very intelligent.

The form teacher, not knowing that I hadn't been privy to the full story, then apologised to me for the way in which my child was treated.

Why was she apologising to me? I wondered.

Wait, what?

My child??

I'd had zero indication from the original complaint that my child had been involved. My kid hadn't mentioned anything about it either.

Being the great parent that I am, with all my well-researched theories and high expectations of my kid, she didn't feel she could talk to me about it.

Yay, look at me, winning the award for Most Approachable Mum.

Real winner.

The form teacher explained that my daughter had asked a question during their library lesson and that the librarian – let's call her Miss Trunchbull – had asked her to stand up and repeat the question several times, only to be told that she needed to 'stop messing around and start paying attention', because that question had already been answered.

I could just picture it:

My 12-year-old, standing in front of the entire class, pigtails quivering, being forced to repeat the question over and over. Each time less sure of herself. Each time more humiliated.

Silly bitch.

Miss Trunchbull, that is.

Not my kid.

You really should never call your kid a bitch.

Not to her face.

Do it in your heart.

Like a winner.

Or you could always substitute the word 'bitch' with 'peach'.

This way, if they have any long-term trauma, you can shirk responsibility on the basis that it was a term of endearment.

Pet name or insult?

Keep 'em guessing.

Keep 'em resilient.

Now, to deal with Miss Trunchbull.

I was determined to keep my cool and handle the matter like the honourable (specifically appointed, ultra-important) Liaison Mum that I was.

I kept my cool for about as long as a cucumber on a braai. The more that I sat with the information, the more I found myself bursting with an overwhelming desire to pour syrup on Miss Trunchbull's chair or put itching powder in her gym knickers.

See how I channelled my inner Matilda? Be kind, but, like, assertive. Also be gracious and courteous. But don't take shit from people. No one likes a shit-eating sub.

I wasn't about to take shit.

My response to Miss Trunchbull was classic Kel. When it comes to standing up for my kid, I won't just make a scene; I will re-enact an entire five-act opus. On top of a soapbox. On the stage of the Royal Opera House.

I immediately called Miss Trunchbull and let her have it.

Miss Trunchbull, as it happened, couldn't understand what she had done wrong and what all the fuss was about.

But if my child's feelings (or those of others in her class) were a measure of her quality as a teacher, she fell decidedly below average.

Wow, someone less average than me!

Or is it more average?

Someone averager than me!

But this conduct would not cut it for me. Not when I was paying exorbitant private school fees.

You should get what you pay for.

The equivalent of a small house.

In the Maldives.

Yes, in addition to the school's manicured grounds, I felt I was entitled to expect teachers who, if not manicured, at least presented themselves fairly towards their students.

Fine, I will get off the stage. But I'm keeping the soapbox.

Because once the curtain fell and the drama simmered down, what stayed with me wasn't the librarian's behaviour. It was the look on my daughter's face when she explained why she hadn't said a word about it to me.

I didn't care about whatever the 'stupid question' was, or Miss Trunchbull's appalling response. What I cared about very much was why my child hadn't stood up for herself. I was also devastated that she hadn't come to me to tell me about what had happened.

When I asked her about it, she said that she had realised that the librarian was feeling stressed, so my daughter didn't feel that it was necessary to 'start drama'.

I don't believe that the onus is on any child to bear the responsibility for an adult's emotions. I was exceedingly proud that my daughter had had the emotional astuteness and compassion to choose sensitivity over retaliation (or an emotional meltdown), but I still thought there was a lesson to be learnt about asserting one's feelings even when someone else is hurting.

While empathy should underpin everything we do, it's still important to teach our children that certain treatment should not be tolerated. In fact, this is a lesson for grownups too. We really cannot go through life taking ownership of any other human's emotions. They are not ours to have.

It's about boundaries.

We need to have the confidence to say, 'I understand your frustration and pain, but the way you have treated me has hurt my feelings. Please do not treat me like this again.'

After the Miss Trunchbull ordeal, I worked together with my daughter on this. We practiced over and over until I was confident that she could say it with conviction.

I was under no illusion that my child was beyond reproach. Well, she was, like, the closest thing to perfect that you could get (except when she didn't listen in class and asked questions which had already been answered).

I was under no illusion that I was beyond reproach as a mum, either. I certainly wasn't a perfect parent, but if my inquisitive, curious, free-thinking, sensitive, perceptive, agreeable, emotionally astute, compassionate daughter was a measure of my quality as a mother, I seemed to be doing alright.

If you just overlooked the fact that she didn't feel she could come to me when she had a problem.

Maybe I needed to focus a little less on Liaison and a little more on Mum, I told myself.

Parenting is about constant recalibration.

So, I recalibrated.

But first I had a little cry.

In my heart.

Like a winner.

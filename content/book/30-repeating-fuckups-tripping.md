# Tripping

I was so enamoured with him, I was tripping.

No, seriously.

Meeting Mr Hot-Afrikaner-Capetonian in person involved cross-country expeditions, careful consideration, and well-thought-out plans.

It only took a series of flights, annual leave, a slew of babysitters, appointments at the local landscaper (to prune overgrown hedges), appointments at the hair salon, appointments at the nail salon, a suitcase bursting with all kinds of clothing (more on this later) and a super-sized box of laxatives for my poo issues.

Piece of cake.

We met in person on several occasions.

A handful of occasions.

Okay, two occasions.

All the places (all two of them) that he booked were quaint and secluded. They were all (all two of them) beautiful, romantic locations.

Private game lodges. Sweet, hidden establishments.

One game lodge.

One hidden establishment.

Why does 'establishment' sound so dodgy? Like a place where people cook meth while hookers doze on torn suede couches. Add 'hidden,' and boom – Guatemala.

But it wasn't Guatemala. It was a chic cozy cabin on the bank of the Magalies River. It boasted a white, carved, four-poster bed, a luxurious en suite bathroom, and a little kitchenette, where I had zero plans to cook (meth or anything else). Through sheer curtains, I could lounge on the huge bed like an empress and overlook the river and bird life. (The bird life was an optional extra. I, personally, had little interest in watching birds).

He spoilt me.

He wouldn't let me pay for anything, even though I tried to insist.

He was so attentive. He also happened to be the first person I dated who was genuinely interested in the wellbeing of my daughter.

He found opportunities regularly to introduce her into conversation. He asked how she was faring at school or with friends, and was interested in my role as a mother.

He made me excited about everything again. I was excited about work, about parenting, about people, and about embracing the joys of life like bedtime texts and minibreaks. I was excited about being in his company and sharing things with him. I was even excited about being vulnerable, although still apprehensive about opening my heart to someone again.

While I was tentative and cautious, testing the waters of this new relationship, he was eager to go all-in. He said that we needed to throw caution to the wind and give it a whirl.

Of course, he didn't actually say, 'give it a whirl.'

No Afrikaner would say, 'give it a whirl', because they're not Pringle-wearing, bicycle-riding Oxbridge alumni with double-barrelled names who read The Telegraph and sip tea in their country houses while living off their posh parents' money. It's not the sort of thing big, burly men in khaki shorts and vellies 'wif' long woollen socks say.

Alright, he didn't actually wear ludicrous 'boere' clothes (though if anyone could have carried them off, he could have). In reality, he dressed like a normal human being.

I'm showing my ancestral British slip here – pun accidental, though apt.

But he still didn't say things like, 'give it a whirl'. He said, 'Ons sal dit doen.'

'We will do it.'

Simple.

I began picturing me and my daughter living in Cape Town. I researched schools for her and places for us to live.

It's important that you start thinking about this 'forever' level of commitment after two minibreaks together.

No less than two, though.

You must only begin to think of HEA after two in-person meets.

Don't be obsessed.

I spent hours attempting to improve my Afrikaans. Trying to speak the language feels like having to clear your throat of a ghastly phlegm buildup. The phonics actually require spit to come to the forefront of your mouth to effectively pronounce them.

Very romantic.

Still, I wanted to impress him. At night, after everyone had gone to bed, I'd sit rehearsing pretend dialogue like it was a school oral:

'Hallo, my naam is Kel. Het ek al genoem dat ek met jou seun wil trou?'

'Did I mention that I want to marry your son?'

Definitely not obsessed.

I downloaded Afrikaans music albums, because there is little else quite as effective as learning a language through song.

If the songs were anything to go by, Afrikaans men were more romantic than English men. These men kiss women in between Bougainvillea plants and stuff. English guys get straight down to business. They skip the flowers and the foreplay and move straight on to fucking in their trucks.

The Afrikaner didn't own a truck.

He owned a Porsche SUV.

With his wealth and stature, the Afrikaner was a sought-after bachelor. Frankly, I didn't give a fig about his money. Ambition is an admirable trait, and his success was a reflection of this, but the actual money meant very little to me. It was his years on me and his experience that appealed to me. His intellect was the kind that was evidently nurtured through time in the saddle, and he was probably the first person I was ever with whom I felt I could grow and learn from.

Initially, I was reluctant to tell my daughter and my folks about him, because I was fearful of their criticism of the prospect of a long-distance relationship. As far as they were concerned, my trips away (all two of them) were work-related.

Then the Afrikaner invited me to visit him in Cape Town.

In his home.

For two whole weeks.

This would be the longest time I had ever been away from my daughter. Ahead of this trip, I felt honour-bound to be truthful to my kid and folks (who would have to look after her while I was away).

I also knew that I needed to let them know where I was going and who I was going with because, in the event that something went wrong, they needed to know where I was and how to get hold of me.

The Afrikaner agreed with me and thought it was a good idea to let people know about our relationship.

For once, I wasn't hiding my relationship behind 'work trips'.

This time, I came clean. I told them that we were not yet sure about a relationship, and that the trip was in aid of deciding whether we could cut it over the distance.

My folks were supremely supportive.

My daughter, not so much.

She had no positive experience of my romantic partners. She was nervous that – once again – her mum would be taken on a rollercoaster ride without her, and that it would ultimately end in turmoil.

I assured her that this guy was different. I also mentioned to her that nothing was cast in stone and that this trip was one of discovery. It was a trip to decide whether we could make a go of a relationship.

She came around with some significant coaxing, in the form of ice cream, in a cup, topped with crushed Oreos.

She came around with a McFlurry.

# The Watermelon

My baby grew beautifully throughout my pregnancy. I also grew beautifully. (Although, arguably, not everyone might consider gaining 24 kilograms beautiful).

I loved feeling my baby kick and have what felt like hiccups. Even though I couldn't share my enjoyment with anyone, the baby's development was something that I held incredibly dear.

If I hadn't felt the baby move in ten minutes, I poked my belly and jumped around. I really loved feeling the little movements inside me. I also didn't know how often unborn babies were supposed to move, and I was terrified that the poor thing was going to die inside my wayward, teenage womb.

Although I was still on my parents' medical insurance scheme, we decided not to have scans at every OB-GYN appointment because they were costly, and so it was only on three occasions that I got to see my baby in utero and affirm that it hadn't kicked the bucket.

At my early appointments, when I was still attending school, I wore my uniform. Great move for inciting judgement.

The judgement was never overt. As in, no one came up to me and said, 'You whore! Why are you here, taking up valuable seating in the OB-GYN waiting room?', but it was fairly obvious, nonetheless.

Raised eyebrows.

Loud sighing.

Clicking of tongues.

Even just averted gazes sent a pretty clear message.

Actually, overt judgement might have been easier to deal with.

The snubbing was stifling. People ignoring me – refusing even to acknowledge my existence – was horrendous.

The doctor would ask questions about my health, about my wellbeing, and about my pregnancy, all through my mum.

'Is she experiencing any pain?'

'Is she still battling with a bladder infection?'

'Is she perhaps eating too much? She appears to have put on more weight than she should have – perhaps we need to cut down on her food intake?'

I wanted to answer the damn questions myself. I wanted to tell the doctor that 'she' was a human being, not a fucking puppy – and that 'she' was right there, for Pete's sake!

In fact, I felt like taking my soapbox out in the waiting room, standing on it in my ill-fitting school uniform, and telling everybody within a kilometre radius that 'she' still – despite her with-child-while-a-child status – was perfectly able to talk and had a fair understanding of how to answer a question, thank you very much.

And, no, 'she' did not have a bladder infection anymore!

Right, now that that's out of the way... where was I?

Oh, yes.

Judgement.

Going to church while pregnant was super fun – except on Sundays.

Twin and Bestie no longer even looked at me. The girls in my grade – most of whom smeared themselves into the Lip Gloss category – were too busy flicking their hair whilst parading up and down the church aisle to come and sit with me in the back row. The elderly ladies with their thick-lensed bifocals and their supremely coiffed hair (which might combust near open flames) scoffed in my direction. And I came to learn that my time at church was best spent praying for forgiveness and for grace.

While I was at it, I also prayed to become invisible.

It wasn't such a big ask, was it? To be made invisible?

I suppose it was difficult when I was growing by the second.

But being invisible seemed a heck of a lot better than being seen through the lens of judgement (bifocal or not).

If people couldn't see me – truly see me – then I didn't want them to see me at all.

Inside my imaginary invisible bubble, I prayed that my baby would be okay.

I prayed that I would be up to the task.

I prayed that God would overlook my mistake and help me to be the mum I needed to be in order to keep my baby.

I prayed that I would be allowed to keep my baby.

As hard as it was to admit to myself, for most part, that decision was not mine to make.

I had no real autonomy over the destiny of the baby that I had created and that I was carrying. Or over my own destiny, for that matter. I didn't know if I would ever get to be called 'Mum'.

There were two couples in the congregation who couldn't have children of their own, and after years of trying had still not managed to fall pregnant. They frequently asked my parents about the possibility of adopting my baby.

They were like fucking hyenas waiting for a buffalo to drop her young so they could hurry in with maniacal cackles and grab it between their teeth.

To be honest, I did feel a bit guilty strutting around parading my obnoxious, superior womb, in the form of a big, fat belly, like, 'Hey, look at me – all fertile and stuff'. My tummy was a glaring reminder of the inadequacy of those who could not do it. People who had planned, longed, and prayed for a baby, whilst I had no plan at all.

It must have seemed very unfair to them.

I think my parents did consider adoption as a viable possibility for a while. Not because that was what they wanted, but because they were battling with their own feelings about the situation. They battled to identify the best decision for me, and for my baby. They battled to navigate the finances associated with the possibility of a new human joining our family.

Our God-appointed pastor (and self-appointed saviour) advised my parents that I needed consequences for my actions. As though the rejection, heartache, and turmoil of teenage pregnancy and single parenting were not consequences enough. He told them that they needed to put their foot (feet? foots?) down. He intimated that I should be turned out of home so that I might atone for my lecherous, horny ways. He felt that I should be forced into giving my baby up.

Spare the rod and all that.

I remember bargaining with God. If He allowed me to keep my baby, I promised, I would behave. I would never ask for a new cell phone or branded T-Shirt again. I'd keep my room clean. I'd study more. I wouldn't backchat my parents (I mean, I would try really hard not to backchat my parents).

I promised God that I would never again pray to be allowed to go to the skating rink or that Twin and Bestie would rezone our relationships.

I promised God that I would close my legs just as soon as my baby arrived.

I promised God that I would do my best to raise and look after this precious gift.

Fortunately for me, my parents could not fathom turning their backs on me and my unborn child. Unfortunately for them, their commitment to me resulted in their also being cast out and judged by some. My folks' friends (the ones who stuck around) constantly made comments like, 'You guys are such amazing parents. I would never be able to support my kid if she had made such a terrible decision as yours did.'

Was this meant to be a compliment?

'You guys are also amazing,' I'd wished my parents would retort. 'Your kid is screwing everything with a penis (and also the occasional vagina), but you raised her to be so good at wrapping that shit up.'

No swearing for them, though.

In hindsight, I wonder whether they ever tried to wish the situation away. You know, like deciding that the best course of action might be the world's most dramatic ostrich impression. Instead of flapping their feathers like they'd just remembered they had an appointment at the panic room, they could have stuck their heads in the sand and hoped the universe would see their 'I'm-not-here' act and be like, 'Oh, they're clearly too busy with their sand nap – let's just let this whole thing slide'.

Even if they maybe secretly considered this POA behind closed doors, my parents decided not to hide their heads in the sand. It was glaringly obvious to me that they made a concerted effort to walk with their heads held high, despite the glares and judgement.

I was proud.

And grateful.

Still, it wasn't as if they handed out cigars or took out a subscription to Your Pregnancy.

In a world of overexposure, I lived this defining chapter of my life behind a veil of polite silence. I was proud – of my parents, of my ability to grow this little human despite the hardship – but I couldn't afford to look it.

Pride, worn publicly, would have offended more sensibilities than the pregnancy itself.

I don't have a single photo of me while I was pregnant. For months, my only identity was 'That Pregnant 16-year-old', yet there is no evidence of my pregnancy. No one could prove that my child wasn't delivered by a stork.

I never had the chance to wear ridiculous maternity lingerie at a 'preggy' photoshoot. I never got to open that stupid curtain thingy in the front of the baby-doll number, lying all sexy-like in an opulent bed while some random person took photos of me, and then have people all over the internet see the grand reveal of my new 'outie' navel.

Actually, thank God I didn't get to do that.

Maybe preggy photos are overrated. When my schoolmates matriculated, then got married, then started having babies (in that all-important order), the stuff on my socials feed was enough to make me nauseous all over again.

Oh dear, I feel a soapbox moment coming on...

Seriously, why don't those Lip Gloss Ladies just wear a pair of leggings and a fitted top and be done with it? What is it with the tacky lingerie or the white boho dresses? And why do they think they need to hold their tummy up with one hand? To ensure it doesn't fall onto their vagina?

And the other hand is always underneath their (now enormous) boobs, framing the extent of their rotundness. Do they feel they have to stack the evidence to prove that they are, indeed, pregnant?

Sweetheart, we saw every single scan and every single pregnancy-progression pic you snapped in front of that mirror (which could do with a wipe, by the way)... week by bloody week. We saw the 300 selfies of your 'condition'. We also saw the wee stick reveal, the gender reveal, the nursery reveal, and the 4D scan reveal – the scan which showed us that 'he looks just like his dad'.

Trust me, we know you're preggos.

Phew. Chuffed I got that off my chest.

On reflection, perhaps it's not the photos that are overrated, just the oversharing of them. I would have loved a couple of pictures to look back on with nostalgia.

The only proof I have that I carried my child are the stretchmarks that travel from my navel to my hoo-ha, my C-section scar (more on that shortly), and the three ultrasound scans which I keep in a special box beside my bed.

Not the soapbox.

Just clarifying.

I had no photos.

No memory board.

No scrapbook.

So, I made my own tribute.

Much to my folks' dismay, I dedicated my entire final art practical to my pregnancy (as one does in matric).

While the other students painted pictures of landscapes and flowers, I painted pictures of my belly.

Some really courageous students painted #woke things that inspired thoughts about gender identity and correct pronouns.

Jokes.

No one did that.

In my day (you can say this when you're 30), you were 'in the know' if you knew the difference between gay, lesbian, and bisexual. Identifying as a cat was never a thing in my day.

Now people are bravely coming out of the proverbial closet (and drawers, and dressers, and bedside tables, and those fancy upholstered ottomans with hidden storage) and owning their vast and various identities.

Well, even back then, I decided to be brave and to 'own' my identity.

Matric art practical, Exhibit A: The Watermelon.

# Breaking Point

For almost three years, I played both parts in a one-sided relationship. I was the provider and the protector; the fighter and the forgiver; the carer and the compromiser.

I was prepared to do all this for love.

I was kidding myself. It wasn't love. It was desperation dressed in endurance.

I convinced myself that I was strong for carrying so much, but I was the weakest I'd ever been. I was just so desperate to keep up the charade of the relationship in the hope that one day he would finally step into the dream.

I totally ignored his indiscretions.

I side-stepped any question which could involve an answer that I did not like.

I made sure never ever to phone him. I only ever texted him because – that way – I couldn't catch him at a bad time and wouldn't have the call cut.

I never showed up unannounced. I always gave him a heads up (even if we had made plans) that I was on my way.

That I was two minutes away.

That I was pulling into his road.

That I was outside his gate.

And so, the navigation of a narcissist continued.

I threw myself, arse over tits, back into the firing line again and again.

Why did I do this? Why did I stay?

Cue soapbox moment.

Okay, it's like this: we think people stay in unhealthy relationships because they are afraid of what will happen to them if they leave.

I'm calling bullshit.

What people don't talk about is the feeling of elation and achievement in flaunting their unwavering commitment. I certainly felt it. I drew strength from my own resilience. I became hooked on affirming my value by my ability to overcome suffering. I honestly felt that I was coming from a position of strength, rather than weakness.

After all, society tells us that the ability to ignore crap and suck it up is a sign of bravery and dedication. Unconditional love and never-ending forgiveness are things to hang one's hat on, things to be proud of.

Right?

Well, boy, could I be proud. I was cheated, hurt, and shoved through obstacle after obstacle, and still I stayed. I was rejected, I was ignored, I was neglected, I was shamed, and still I remained steadfast.

Who's the narcissist now?

Marathon runners are prepared to lose toenails, and years off their life, because they're committed to an end goal.

Absolutely, unconditionally committed.

Maybe they should be committed to an asylum.

Nevertheless, I became similarly obsessed, similarly driven towards my own goal of keeping Tinder Guy faithful. If I only stuck it out a little longer, he would choose me. He would see how strong and loyal and wonderful I was.

I depended on this to reaffirm my self-worth.

That was the reason I stayed.

That was the reason I continued.

I knew that our relationship was toxic, but I kept at it.

It was like smoking – I was vividly aware of exactly what it did to my lungs, but that didn't stop me from consuming a pack a day when I could afford it.

Of course, I regularly tried to break the ghastly habit, but always went back to it again.

That's what it was like with Tinder Guy.

I tried to break the harmful addiction.

I tried to quit.

I even blocked him on WhatsApp.

I deleted his number.

But I had his number imprinted on my brain, so it was easy to resave it and unblock him again.

Block.

Unblock.

Block.

Unblock.

Block.

Unblock.

Like a temperamental toilet. And just as full of crap.

It was flushing my dignity away.

But, you see, I convinced myself that I needed to unblock him.

There were reasons.

Like if I needed to send him a text to wish him 'Happy birthday.'

Or 'You forgot your sweater at my house, and I need to return it.'

You know, just to give him the opportunity to reply. Or to tell me that he missed me and wanted me back.

The real reason was that I was still seeking his validation.

Deep in my gut (where no one wants to look), I knew this was madness. I knew that I needed to get over him and to move on.

Everything depended on it. My daughter depended on me to do it.

I had to break the addiction before it broke me. Since willpower clearly wasn't working, I needed to come up with another solution.

Like with any addict, I understood that the best course of action would be to remove myself from the environment where I could easily access the addiction.

I decided to send myself to emotional rehab.

I decided to move back home.

I gave notice on the lease on my little cottage, and I returned to my folks' house, cap in hand, feeling like such a failure.

My furniture, my fridge, my washing machine, my towels and linen, my Carol Boyes kitchenware – everything I had gotten into so much debt for – was put into storage. At the low-water mark of my life, just when I felt like the biggest failure of a human being, I had to seal the boxes on everything that looked like accomplishment to me, and move back into my childhood bedroom.

My daughter moved into my brother's old bedroom, and we both became – essentially – children under my folks' roof.

Again.

It was tough.

Not because it was a horrible place to live, but because I was back where I'd struggled as a teenage mum, and every room, every piece of furniture, every cushion cover and teaspoon was a subtle reminder of the emotions of that time.

Subtle as a howler monkey. In mating season.

I was completely disillusioned and disenchanted with life.

It was, without a doubt, the best decision I ever made.

Being in a supportive environment with people who had my best interests at heart was a refreshing change of scenery. Emotional security, and having someone else do my laundry, also both did me a world of good.

This gave me more time and energy to focus on building my relationship with my daughter.

I really paid attention to her, and she lapped it up. Especially at bedtime, because the clever clogs realised that the more she talked, the longer it would be until I forced her to go to sleep.

Having put in a full day at work and helped with homework, housework, cooking, and cleaning up, all I wanted to do was fall onto the couch and watch Netflix. Some nights I was so damn desperate for her to get to the end of her story so that I could do that, that I nodded and 'mm-hmm'ed and 'wow'ed her into a frenzy.

Encouraged by my attentiveness, she manifested and embellished all avenues of the account, and what started off as a story about her day soon became some fucked-up kid version of Vikings, with parallel narratives, hectic sound effects, and all-round massacres.

Only, with her story (because I was wise and did not let my nine-year-old watch Vikings), the things massacred were my brain cells and my will to breathe.

I exaggerate. I adored listening to my daughter's stories.

The bonus was that – when she was barrelling into the fourth conclusion of her evening anecdote – I didn't have time to think about my own story, my own misery, my own relational demise.

I began to pick up the pieces of my broken heart and move on with life.

I stopped seeing Tinder Guy.

Then I stopped texting him.

After a while, I even stopped stalking him on social media (mostly).

It took a long time before I stopped imagining that I would receive the 'grand gesture' from him, though.

I thought of him often, and it still hurt, but I stopped giving him as much mental real estate. Each time my brain was determined to take the treacherous trip down memory lane, I made a conscious detour.

I had reached a breaking point, in more ways than one. I was already regularly breaking out in cold sweats, rashes, and tears. I'd broken the bank, I'd broken the mould, now I had to break the spell.

I knew that I had to get out of there before I broke into a million pieces (always dramatic, me). I needed to make a break for it. It was a make-or-break situation. I'd been breaking my back trying to keep Tinder Guy faithful, and it clearly hadn't worked.

I'm just lucky that my family were there to break my fall.

And – as I came to learn – in my decision to try to break my addiction to this toxic relationship, I was also giving myself a break.

Taking the pressure off not only decreased my immediate stress levels; it gave me the space to broaden my focus. I could give more of my attention to other areas of my life, like my work, my self-esteem, and – especially – my daughter.

It wasn't a clean break, but it was a necessary one.

On balance, I figured I'd succeeded in breaking even.

As long as I didn't break wind, I could consider it a victory.

The one lingering problem was the loneliness. I still longed for a special someone to share my life with, but I sure as nuts wasn't going back on Tinder. That app was not 'appening'!

In an attempt to resuscitate my ailing social life, and maybe even meet someone, I accepted invites from friends and acquaintances, even when I really didn't feel like it.

By this stage, most of my peers had tied the knot and were settling into married life. Initially, faced with this at social gatherings, I felt a deep sense of yearning for someone to be by my side. These people's apparent wedded bliss only served to highlight my singleness. Each one of my friends' husbands took me on as an auxiliary wife. Some even introduced me to their other friends as their 'second wife'.

While it was sweet, it did absolutely nothing to stop the yearning.

I also longed for someone to make sure my steak was cooked medium-rare instead of torched by some random who treated braai duty like it was a group project – i.e. hoping someone else would do all the work.

Apparently, having a vagina (no matter how good it looks) disqualifies me from using fire tongs. But that's a soapbox for another day.

As I observed my couple friends, I realised something. Couples fight about the obvious stuff: unmade beds, dinged car doors, incorrect dishwasher loading, mystery charges on the credit card.

The thing is, though, that when you're not in a relationship, you can't fixate on these quantifiable problems. There's no one else to blame for the messy bed, the car scratches, the dishwasher, or the mystery purchases.

(Confession: even with no one around to bust me, I still squirrelled away shopping bags at the top of my cupboard. Sure, it was crazy. But surprise stilettos in September? Worth it.)

When there's no one to reproach for the external circumstances of your life, you're forced to face the internal issues – which have probably been driving all the others issues anyway.

Backseat drivers, the little fuckers.

Only once you're no longer preoccupied with another person, can you deal with matters like at what point your self-worth became so diminished that it you allowed yourself to be subjected to such crap.

In a relationship, I'd only had to worry about the devil I knew.

Now I began to wonder if I was the devil I didn't.

It certainly became apparent that I didn't know myself very well.

Perhaps unsurprising, when I'd grafted my identity onto a moving target. A skipping scapegoat.

Was it all my fault, then?

Was I responsible for the hell in which I found myself?

Was I honestly my own Satan?

I certainly couldn't pull off the sexy Dark Angel look. So, if I was my own Satan, it would have to be without the horns and high heels.

What was diabolical, I realised, is that I'd allowed my self-esteem to get so low. Surely I deserved better?

I looked around at my married friends and imagined myself with a partner who loved and respected me. And cooked my steak properly.

It was a nice thought. I just hoped it wasn't a pipe dream.

Life was definitely on the up, though I still got into bed feeling super lonely every night.

But I came to the conclusion that I would take actually being alone over feeling lonely in a relationship every day of the week.

People told me to 'hang in there.'

That there was someone 'out there' for me.

That he was probably 'just around the corner.'

That it would happen 'when I least expected it.'

So I went about my business, focusing really hard on least expecting it.

'Don't mind me. I'm just over here, carrying on. Feel free to happen, since I am not even remotely expecting it.'

There I was, diligently not expecting it. And just when I truly, finally didn't... it happened.

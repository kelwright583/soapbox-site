# Failing Fabulously

Twenty kilograms.

I gained 20 kilograms after the Afrikaner's snaky exit.

Twenty fucking kilograms in the time that I was trying to rebuild the 'foundations' of my spiritual 'home'.

Well, nothing screams 'homely' like becoming a right porker.

But 20 kilograms was nothing compared to the weight of the emotional crapola that I had let go.

I went from a size 8 to a size 14. I traded in my bras for gargantuan DD-cup models that could double as a pair of parachutes in an emergency. You know, in case I ever found myself skydiving with a stranger who forgot which cord to pull.

(Of course, I didn't get rid of the smaller, cuter bras. I kept them – along with the smaller jeans, tops, and blazers – for that undesignated future time when I might, maybe, perhaps fit into them again.)

It wasn't that I purposefully opted for binge-eating pizza, burgers, and ice cream.

I mean, I totally did all of that – with the enthusiasm of a glutton who recently got home from fat camp.

But it wasn't like I avoided salads to make a point.

I hadn't particularly wanted to gain the weight. It just kind of happened.

And the crazy, wonderful thing was: it didn't bother me all that much.

Well, having to buy a whole new wardrobe of box tops and granny jeans on zero budget wasn't wonderful. Trying to shove my spare tyre into my new, super-high-waisted granny jeans was also shitty.

But I found satisfaction in the newly discovered contentment that, actually, it didn't bloody matter.

So I was borderline heifer. So my cheeks looked as though they were storing nuts for the winter. So I had boob coming out of every part of my bra (including the back).

Side-boob, top-boob, under-boob, back-boob.

Left-boob, right-boob, boob-in-stereo.

This could be a song.

It could make the next radio Top 40.

It could become the anthem of a new generation of empowered, unapologetically real women who honour their bodies and rewrite the rules of self-worth.

I could be rich and famous and retire in peace to some tropical island where my boobs could loll around in (and out) of a bikini while I sip cocktails and read raunchy novels.

Well, that didn't happen.

Pity.

But I did embrace my new body.

I embraced body positivity like a long-lost loved one.

I must admit that it was oddly satisfying, being content in all my cuddliness.

I was focused on finding home within myself, remember. And now I had plenty of room to build one. With large garden. And a pool. And a tennis court.

The situation compelled me to look deeper than my appearance for cues on my value.

*mounting soapbox (this time with some huffing and puffing)*

I gave myself grace for previously viewing every partner's lack of reciprocal love as a challenge to convince them (and myself) of my worth.

I learnt that I didn't need to be skinny, with a tidy vagina. I didn't need to be rich, or hitched, or have my own home filled with Carol Boyes kitchenware.

I finally realised that my value lay in so many other factors.

I was really quite sharp, when I didn't let my insecurities get in my own way. I was astute. I was able to get a fairly good read on people, on situations, and (eventually) on myself. I realised that I had a gift for being perceptive. But we knew this. I'd always had this gift; I just hadn't always listened to it.

I wasn't so average in the intellectual department, either. I could navigate a conversation on pretty much any topic. I could formulate solutions to difficult problems. Remember, I got bloody straight A's for matric (except for that wily Afrikaans fucker) after missing six months of school and caring for a newborn. I completed a degree and two diplomas while dodging and ducking some serious uppercuts life had thrown at me. I managed to maintain a very prestigious, ultra-important, super-adventurous, not-boring-at-all position at my company despite the silly, silly boys who traipsed in and out of my life.

My sense of humour became something I flipping loved about myself. I found that I had an innate ability to find the funny in almost anything, regardless of how serious or depressing it was. I think this became apparent because I no longer took myself so damn seriously.

I mean, how could I? I was 30, a single mum to a teenager, living with the 'roommates' (AKA parents), and had no assets to speak of.

No money.

No picket fence.

No carat.

What I did have was a podgy cooch.

As a side note: I really think this whole fat fanny thing is a fundamental human design flaw. (I'm going to write an essay of complaint.)

Taking life more lightly helped me find more joy in the everyday things. It found it hilarious that I got an entire workout just getting dressed in the morning. I had to jump, wriggle, and cavort in order to get my pants over my calves... then my thighs... then my bottom, which now resembled a bulldozer in size and probably should have come with beep-beeps, in case I ever needed to walk backwards.

('Beep-beeps' is, of course, the technical automotive term for a vehicular reverse warning alarm system.)

I found it fucking hysterical when the full dancing, cavorting routine resulted in me losing my balance, toppling over, and landing with a thud on the floor – and then battling to get up because my pants were still stuck around my kankles and so it looked like I was engaged in an exceptional horizontal penguin impression.

Have you ever experienced what it is like to laugh at yourself, and your body literally laughs along with you? It feels like the ripples of joy are jiggling through your body, partaking in the best kind of portly party, and your fat rolls are the perfect participants.

Not only was I perceptive, smart, and funny, I acknowledged, but I was a good mum. That was another thing I discovered about myself when I pulled my head out of my enormous bottom.

Good parenting has little to do with remembering to buy posterboard and craft glue or producing dietician-approved meals three times a day. It doesn't mean always purchasing the latest clothes or expensive gadgets. It's about loving your kid as hard as you can. And I loved my daughter with an intensity that couldn't be articulated. (Let's face it – if it could be articulated, I would have been the one to find the words to do so.)

I was a good mum because she was healthy. She was well adjusted. She was fed, clothed, lived in a safe, comfortable space, and was educated, supported, encouraged, and loved. And not because I achieved all that single-handedly (and nearly drove myself to burnout in the process to prove a point), but because I learnt to accept the help offered by those around us who loved us. I put her in environments (most of the time) where she could get all those things from a tribe of people, even if I couldn't always do them myself.

When all the superficial bullshit was erased and the focus was no longer on how I could create the best portrayal of success, I found enjoyment in myself, and in all the things I did have going for me.

Things I didn't have to sweat for.

I stopped measuring myself by other people's yardsticks and started laying bricks on my own terms.

The 'concrete' for the foundations of my new (albeit more substantial) home was starting to set. Without cracks. Without spalling. Without bowing. It was strong.

I was strong.

I came to appreciate my innate traits, like my ability to care, my generosity, and my kindness. It would have been easy to conclude that my problem was that I simply cared too much. Maybe if I hadn't cared, or hadn't committed to certain people, or hadn't invested in them, I wouldn't have gotten so terribly hurt.

People actually told me that, you know.

'Your problem is that you care too much. You love too hard. You invest too much.'

I realised, though, that those were the things that made me who I was.

I was proud to stand on my symbolic soapbox and triumphantly proclaim that I didn't want to ever have to stop caring and loving and investing in people, including myself.

That's what life's all about, and that's what I needed to teach my daughter.

My ability (or lack thereof) to be thin, to be fit, to make a packet of cash, or to lock down a successful relationship meant absolutely nothing to her. She was thriving, and this was one of the only benchmarks I needed for success.

I wasn't a failure. I was fabulous!

Or I was failing fabulously. But it really didn't matter, because at long last I realised that all that truly mattered in life was to love, to laugh, and to find joy wherever we could.

And this was me loving myself at my 'worst.' How exciting then, how rewarding, how absolutely gratifying would it be, when I got to experience myself at my best?

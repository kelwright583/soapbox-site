# Apples and Trees

While I'd been navigating all my emotional peaks and troughs, my daughter (then teetering on 12 years old) was doing some fairly wild navigations of her own. First up, she was dealing with her changing body. She no longer looked like a little girl; she was staggering inelegantly into womanhood, and she was gaining awareness of her own sense of self.

Second, she was developing in different ways. Among other things – like intelligence and wit and a vast multitude of opinions – she also developed a ferocious teenage mumble.

The murmuring thing.

That thing that teens do when they talk under their breath.

That thing that makes you want to smack them in the mouth.

I wouldn't, obviously.

I am, like, an excellent mum and stuff.

I thought about it though.

Once.

(ish.)

My daughter mumbled retorts to me if I made a comment she didn't enjoy. She mumbled her dissatisfaction about her friends' social faux pas, her homework, and her bedtime. She mumbled if she didn't like what was for dinner. But more than anything, I noticed, she mumbled shit about her body. Shit that sounded an awful lot like the shit I mumbled about mine.

It was starting to get on my big, saggy tits.

But who could blame her?

She was learning from my very own playbook. My way of handling my low self-image was to frequently make stupid jokes about my body and run myself down. She was taking her cues from me.

I'd always battled with what I looked like. It now became clear that my kid shared the same struggles.

The apple certainly didn't fall far from this tree.

It fell face-first into the bullshit we're fed about our bodies, through the lens of a world obsessed with everything that adds value to neither man nor beast.

I thought back to my body issues dating back to my youth: Mrs C Ruel and her callous comments. The board shorts. The shame.

I now realised that I needed to do some serious work on my own self-esteem if I didn't want my daughter to inherit my issues of insecurity and quiet agony about her body.

I had a belated epiphany.

Wait, wait! Soapbox!

Hear ye, hear ye! Come one, come all to hear the wisdom of Kel!

(It's easy to be wise when you've had a sudden psycho revelation.)

(Did I say psycho? I meant psychic.)

(I think.)

Here's the revelation: we often interpret things that might not actually be true.

Why, for instance, when someone stares at us in our swimsuits, might we immediately assume that they are judging us? Why doesn't it occur to us that maybe they are looking at us in awe? Why do we never consider that they might be thinking, 'Gosh, if only I could pull off a bikini like that!'

And even if people are silently judging us, we don't have to believe it. It's not like we'll ever talk to them about it to find out the truth.

Benefit of the doubt and all that.

We have the capacity to create our own truth. A truth that sits well with us. A truth that promotes confidence and love of ourselves.

One day my kid came home and bitched in ruffled mumbles that So-and-so didn't like her. It made me want to scream (and go out and garrot the obnoxious So-and-so in a dark alley).

'Why doesn't So-and-so like you?' I probed.

'Dunno.'

'Did she tell you she doesn't like you?'

'Nphm.'

'Then what makes you think she doesn't like you?'

'Mmfdndmmf,' she muttered as she skulked out the room towards her room.

SLAM.

My ears and my heart hurt.

Granted, I had a few years on her to develop my opinions on these matters, yet it killed me to see her sabotaging her own self-worth. I could see exactly where this was going, and I didn't like it.

I felt I had to weigh in on the subject (pun categorically intended), but since my daughter had left me standing on my metaphorical soapbox like a lone sock after laundry day, I will have to be content with enlightening you about my revelation instead, in the hope that it will be of use to you or someone you love.

I ask: why choose to believe that someone doesn't like you?

Did someone tell you that? Tell me straight to my face that someone came up to you and said, 'Hey, you there, average human. I don't like you.'

I doubt it.

There are few people on this earth who have the level of courage (or self-importance) to explicitly run you down. So why not choose to believe that they actually think you are marvellous?

Would it hurt?

We don't have to stick our heads so far in the sand that the sun bleaches our bum holes, but what would be the harm in choosing, at first blush, to assume the best?

If no one overtly tells you that you suck, why choose to believe that you do, or that they think that you do?

The harsh truth is that people are too consumed with themselves to give you much thought. While this is particularly true of teenagers, even as adults, the same logic applies.

Think about it. When you go to the supermarket, are you focused on anyone else, or are you too busy being shocked by the price of fucking avos, or bothered by the fact that your knickers are once again rock-climbing up your bottom? You probably hardly notice anyone else. So why do you think that they would notice you? Why would you think they (whoever they are) would notice that you wore the same shirt last time you were at the shop, let alone judge you for it?

They might be looking in your direction to check the price of a can of tuna on the shelf behind you. They're not necessarily skeefing you and calling you 'fatso' in their heads.

We need to stop getting offended so easily, for Pete's sake.

Also, for our own sake.

But mostly, for our daughters' sake.

I finally stopped caring about what other people thought of me – positive or negative.

I'd spent most of my life continuously choosing to consume a diet of filth and of nonsense telling me what the 'perfect' body/face/hair/nails/vagina should look like. Yes, it had been my choice. I could stand up here, blaming the Lip Gloss Ladies, and the expectations of the boys around me, and the media, and society in general, but ultimately none of them could physically hack into my skull and manually change my mind. I'd chosen to think that way. I had to own those choices.

As with any diet, we have a choice about what to ingest. When presented with a salad of spinach and strawberries, I can simply throw the strawberries away. (Fruit in salad should be a crime. Except for tomato, which identifies as veg.)

I appear to have gotten sidetracked. Why was I standing up here again? Flip man, ageing is a bitch. I'm only 30, damnit! Next thing I know, I'll find myself standing at the fridge with my toothbrush and the garden hose and wondering what I'm doing there.

Oh, yes, I remember!

I realised that at least part of my daughter's poor internal dialogue was on me. It was bad enough that I'd missed out on opportunities in life because of my own self-imposed shame. But the real kicker was that I could see my daughter embarking on the same path.

She 'forgot' her swimming costume for PE lessons at school because she didn't want people to see her legs. She hid her body under layers of clothing – even in summer – because the broad message she received from society was that her body did not fit the picture of 'perfect'.

I told her countless times that her body was perfect as-is. Not 'flawless'. Not necessarily 'slim' or 'smooth'. But perfect nonetheless, because it was divinely designed especially for her.

Unfortunately, my own lack of body confidence had been louder than any affirmation. I wasn't setting a very good example (what ever happened to the 'do as I say, not as I do' school of parenting?).

I realised that I needed to get out of her way. If the apple was going to fall close to this tree, I had better make sure the tree wasn't casting a perilous shadow.

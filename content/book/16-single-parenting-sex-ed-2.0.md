# Sex Ed 2.0

I was navigating the whole mum-gig like a champion.

As in, I got my kid to school on time. I did her homework with her and signed her homework book (most days). I remembered school projects, sports days, and parent-teacher interviews (at least, I did when my mum reminded me). I also started developing a vehement stance on all things mama and bear (sometimes with a sore head).

My daughter was in Grade 2 when she got her first iPad. This was a school requirement, otherwise she'd never have gotten one so soon (they were so expensive that I was already considering selling my body to keep her at the private school).

She loved the iPad. I was amazed at how quickly she picked up all the tech stuff.

If the Nokia 3310 could handle WhatsApp, I'd still be using it. I lost my shit when iPhone decided to scrap the perfectly functional fingerprint reader and replace it with the finicky, hypersensitive facial recognition software that only acknowledges me when I'm wearing glasses and otherwise acts like I'm a total stranger.

Yet my kid, at eight, could download apps, pick up assignments on Google Classroom, and research anything on Safari.

It served her well at school.

Until it didn't.

One morning, in the middle of the first term, I received an alarming phone call.

'Hello, Miss Wright. I'm phoning from your daughter's school,' the secretary announced in her nasally voice.

My stomach immediately contracted in on itself like a pufferfish in reverse.

'Everything is fine,' the nasally voice continued. 'But would you mind perhaps dropping in to see the headmistress?'

'Um, sure,' I squeaked, scrambling for my diary and grabbing my stomach with my other hand. 'How will 12 pm work?'

'I think it would be best if you came right now,' declared the secretary.

My stomach promptly fell out of my bottom.

My first thought was: my daughter has broken her leg.

My second thought was: how much money is it going to cost me at the emergency room?

Do I even have the money?

I don't have her medical records on me – what if she is allergic to something that they give her at the ER?

What if she goes into anaphylactic shock?

What if her heart stops?

Will they make me step out the room while they resuss her?

Okay, too far.

Stay on track, Kel!

The leg.

Focus on the broken leg.

Shit.

How am I going to get her out of the car, find a wheelchair, and take her into the emergency room all at the same time that I need to park my car?

Where will I park?

Will I need cash for the parking machine?

Oh, fuck! Where the bloody hell is the emergency room, anyway??

'Miss Wright?' the secretary rudely interrupted my thoughts.

'Uh, yes! I'm on my way!'

I arrived at her school – after breaking the sound barrier, never mind the speed limit – and was directed to the headmistress's office.

Outside, in the waiting area, I found my baby with a tear-stained face, wringing her hands with obvious anxiety. She was sitting in a wicker chair, in her very cute blue and white striped pinafore, her little legs swinging nervously beneath her.

Two legs.

Both intact.

Phew.

I gave her a hug to try to console her, and waited with her until we were called in to the office.

In the meeting, I was informed that another parent had complained because my daughter had exposed her child to some 'unsavoury' content. This came after a dictionary exercise the previous day, in which the class was instructed to research words starting with the letter S. Some of the girls had apparently decided to look up the word 'sex' on their iPads. My daughter was obviously intrigued, and – being the inquisitive, curious, free-thinker that I'd raised her to be – had done a light recon of the subject matter.

Within eyeshot of the precious pudding whose mother had called that morning to complain.

I hadn't seen this coming, because I'd considered it a bit early to be having the 'birds fucking bees' talk.

Just great.

It turned out that, after the secretary had phoned me, my daughter had been summoned to the headmistress's office and taken to task for her reprehensible conduct. The headmistress had called me in to inform me about the 'incident' and to explain that it had been 'dealt with'.

I swallowed the tirade rapidly rising in my throat.

You mean to tell me that you ring-fenced my eight-year-old, sent her to the headmistress's office – the same office that is threatened mercilessly as the ultimate punishment for poor behaviour – and had a conversation with her about her first ever exposure to sex, without my knowledge and without the school counsellor present?

And it was necessary that I drop everything that I was doing in the middle of the working day and rush to the school to intervene, only to find that you'd already 'dealt with' it without me?

Well, fuck.

I wished I'd brought my soapbox, but I hadn't thought about it in my haste. Because, you know, this meeting was so damn urgent.

Well, I have it now, folks, and I am preparing to stand on it with gusto. Those of you in the back, this is important. Someone hand me a mic.

Is it any wonder that children are so fascinated and fearful about sex? We tell them that it's good to ask questions, to explore things, to broaden their horizons, to do research, and to educate themselves – but then, when they do this with regard to sex, we castigate them.

We would be proud of our kids if we saw that they had been researching 'how aeroplanes work' or 'ancient civilisations', yet somehow this pride vanishes when they research topics which are probably more essential to their lives, and we avoid dealing with the issue by taking away tablets and devices which could be used to teach valuable lessons.

How about, rather than being angry because they stumbled across some hair-raising (or crotch-raising) content, we grownups talk to them about what they've seen?

I am not for a second proposing that parents should whip out some popcorn and watch porn with their children, but I am suggesting that we need to teach them skills in safe internet usage.

Imagine if we took the time and the guts to sit with them and guide them on the types of things they should avoid when researching. How about we reassure them when they feel shocked or afraid, because they probably had no intention at all of stumbling upon this horrendous subject matter in the first place? How about we acknowledge that society is to blame for this content, not them?

How about we google, with them, child-friendly explanations of sex? How about we build a safe environment, and a relationship based on trust, so that our children feel comfortable to talk to us, rather than complain to us, if and when they see something that makes them confused or intrigued.

What if we changed the narrative, and instead of being so bogged down by the actual content, we relied on equipping them with the skills to process it all?

An 'incident' like this could be a flipping phenomenal launchpad for a really cool conversation about personal boundaries, healthy behaviour, or how (and why) they might make different choices in future.

It is devastating when innocence is lost as a result of exposure to explicit sexual content, but it is equally devastating when children do not learn the skills to turn off videos that conflict with their values.

It's impossible to ensure that they are not subjected to any form of distaste throughout their childhood. While there need to be some protective parameters, I believe that – in an increasingly technological world – we are setting our children up for failure if we do not encourage safe exposure to technological devices and the web of knowledge that they open up.

Would you send your kid off to camp without insect repellent?

Well, sending her off into today's world without technological know-how, or the confidence to deal with content, will result in far worse maladies than itchy bites.

Keeping kids excessively sheltered is unfair on them. If we don't allow our children to have opinions, they can't forge their own beliefs, nor will they acquire the necessary competence and confidence to articulate themselves, their values, and any groundbreaking discoveries they may make.

While I feel that my fab parenting style is totally fortifying the type of child who will grow up equipped for adulthood and the plethora of life's ridiculous uppercuts, there are times that I am met with the odd contrasting opinion.

Okay, most of the time.

And a lot of contrasting opinions.

Also, it's probably just me that thinks my parenting style is fab.

Nonetheless...

I am pro-iPads, pro-exposure, pro-discussion, pro-questioning.

Hell, I am even pro-boundary-pushing.

I am not pro-punishment, but I am pro-discipline.

(I reserve the right to change my mind should I ever have a kid who is not as sensitive, perceptive, and agreeable as my daughter is.)

Basically, I don't believe that I have any command over another human being. I have no more control over a two-year-old than I have over a 22-year-old. Or a 102-year-old.

I believe that parenting should be about guiding without agenda, moulding without coercion, and loving without manipulation. I have never had, nor will I ever have, complete control over my kid's decisions or behaviour. My role is to offer her guidance and love, and a safe, supportive environment in which to explore her options. Her decisions, though, should be entirely hers. Hopefully, the byproduct of this will be a child who grows up to be a kind, resilient, contributing member of society.

We could look at it this way: raising a kid is like nurturing a seedling.

(Before we go on, I must point out that this is completely allegorical. If someone gave me an actual seedling, it would die a certain and fairly sudden death.)

But back to the metaphor. The way I see it, my responsibility is to put my little seedling into fertile soil, water her, and make sure she gets enough sunlight (and, on occasion, allow her to experience the other elements, within the boundaries of what she can withstand at the time).

Or to give her sugar-free Coke on nights when the Golden Arches is the solid alternative for meat and three veg.

Her life choices – and what sort of flower or tree she grows into as a result – are entirely up to her.

Unless she decides to become Ronald McDonald. In this case, the decision will be up to me.

*mic drop*

That was a supremely long-winded soapbox moment. Probably wise that I get back on track.

Where was I?

Oh, the headmistress.

Back in her office, the headmistress reassured me that she had had a 'little chat' with my daughter, and that the reason I'd been called in so urgently was to discuss taking my kid to a psychologist and to recommend no less than eight sessions to 'get to the bottom of her behaviour'.

Cute.

Bloody hell.

Actually, bring back that soapbox.

For the sake of expediency, I will attempt to practice a semblance of brevity:

For starters, do not send my kid to the headmistress's office to have this specific 'chat'. The school had plenty of other rooms which could have been used – classrooms which were not associated with fear and punishment.

Secondly, I paid premium fees for private education, and one of the benefits was that the school had trained counsellors available who could have been called upon to navigate this process. Use them!

Thirdly, do not phone me, have me shit my pantsuit because I need to get to the school 'right now', and then have me arrive to find that you took on the matter without me anyway.

Finally, I'd suggest that you don't suggest psychology sessions if you've got no intention of paying for them. Also, because, that's a pretty fucking shit suggestion.

I assertively made this case to the headmistress.

Jokes.

This was private school. I smiled, nodded, and did absolutely nothing that could be described as assertive.

Being a private school mum is mostly about keeping up appearances. I had to maintain the illusion that I respected the school's way of doing things.

I said, 'Absolutely. I see where you are coming from.'

And then I sent my kid to only six shrink sessions.

Rebel that I am.

So yeah, they gave my daughter a questionable education.

And I gave the school a little Sex Ed of my own.

Not really.

But in my head, it was a masterclass.

# Full-Arsing It

When my daughter was about three years old, I made a concerted effort to try out this 'swanning' business for real, and to get my life back on track. I traded in my job at the pub for a more permanent position at a car dealership as a Finance and Insurance Assistant (it sounds fancier when it's capitalised). I committed to finishing my degree, and signed up for further courses and diplomas.

I joined a gym (and actually used my overpriced membership) to get my body back in shape. I ate healthily. I took on competitive hobbies, such as hockey and trying to outshine fellow parents at the school bake day. I worked hard because I wanted a big house and a big car that could mount a pavement (and it would be great if it had a sunroof, too). I ensured that my nails weren't chipped, and my hair roots were done. I planned play dates. I gave children a reasonable amount of money for birthday gifts. I pulled off sensational parties for my own kid's birthdays. I remembered Mother's Day, Father's Day, Freedom Day, Teacher's Day, and all the other important days, like Wednesday...

Let it never be said that I do anything half-arsed. I always use my full arse.

Somehow, I went from having little to no hopes for myself to having outlandish expectations of myself.

Perhaps I was still trying to atone for my myriad mistakes. Or at least trying to prove that I was a force to be reckoned with, in the hope that people would take me seriously. I desperately wanted to be worthy of the title 'precious'. And, because I have no sense of proportion, I figured that if I was going to be 'precious,' I wanted to be the platinum edition. And I full-arsed my efforts towards earning the title.

I became one of those people who thought that juggling hundreds of balls was impressive.

You know the type: 'So stressed. Just so busy.'

#stressed

#stressedbutblessed

#toobusy

#struggleisreal

Where is my soapbox?

We all appear to have subscribed to the bullshit that dictates that stress deserves a badge of honour, and that the more drama we can embed ourselves in, the more valuable we are.

Did I mention that this is utter bullshit?

Righto, glad to have made it clear. My soapbox can now be temporarily stowed.

In my endeavour to atone for my lechery and recklessness, I fell into the trap of placing my value in my ability to endure, my ability to juggle balls (hockey, metaphorical, testicular), my ability to look and play the part of 'she who is not so average', and my ability to do all of this without help from anyone.

I wanted to show the world that I could overcome all the adversity by sheer fortitude.

It took me some time to figure out that I would be better able to reach my full potential if I accepted assistance from others, and that to be the best mum I could be, I had to let go of the things I did badly and leave them up to those who did them well. In fact, when I looked at the days that went really well with my daughter, they usually included a helping hand or two.

Allowing my mum to soothe my kid when I needed a break, letting my folks pay for things I could not afford, saying no to making more cupcakes than was necessary... these were things I had to learn to do to be my best self (and therefore the best parent, best daughter, best Finance and Insurance Assistant, etc that I could be).

It was an important life lesson: we need to learn to accept more help.

Outsource.

Delegate.

However, there are some exceptions to this rule.

*unstows soapbox*

Breastfeeding is not one of the things we should delegate. Don't let others breastfeed your kid – there is no room for wet nurses in this day and age. Also, dry nurses. Also, any kind of nurse that means your kid must have their mouth on someone else's nipple.

Formula was invented for a reason. #justsaying.

*restows soapbox*

Accepting aid not only enabled me to thrive, but it availed me of the opportunity to work toward being the kind of mum I wanted to be, and to establish a healthy relationship with my child. When basic survival was taken care of through the help of others, I was able to focus on the things that I was truly excellent at.

Okay, excellent is an embellishment.

Things I was slightly above average at.

Being able to focus on the things I did well gave me the confidence I needed to persevere. I clung to each small win. Every time I successfully managed to get her down for a nap or have her bathed and ready for bed at a reasonable hour or had a fun play session with her, I held onto the win.

Each small achievement reaffirmed my worth as her mum.

In those moments, I stopped measuring myself by what I lacked, and started seeing the impact of my abundant love for her. With an increased sense of self-worth, I felt empowered to keep on working toward providing for her, toward being established and successful.

Success is just as sweet if you get there with someone's help. Like when someone soothes your screaming baby.

(Not by breastfeeding, though.)

I realised that I could count myself as a winner even if I wasn't great at soothing my wailing kid in the midst of a high temperature, while my mother was bloody good at it. My worth was not diminished because I never quite got the hang of breastfeeding, or because I didn't have a shit-ton of money to get the latest baby monitor/car/duplex in a shmancy neighbourhood. My value was not thwarted because I had to ask for financial assistance.

Neither was my value increased in any way by trial or adversity.

White-knuckling my way through life did not give me a badge of honour.

It gave me bloody arthritis.

Or at least it would have, had I kept that shit up.

So I learnt slowly (sometimes the hard way, through trial and error) to find my balance between independence and accepting assistance.

Sometimes I still get it wrong.

What an arse.

But then, that's me.

Full-arsing my way through life.

Like I said, I don't do half-arsed. If life requires effort, I'm not giving it half my arse. I came fully loaded.

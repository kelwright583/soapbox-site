# Burst

It wasn't just a bubble.

It was an overinflated balloon, its edges trembling with energetic potential.

And, on a sunny Sunday morning in June, it popped.

About five months into my relationship with Tinder Guy, one of my closest friends was a spectator at that year's Comrades Marathon.

(I can't understand why someone would give up her Sunday to sit on the side of a national highway and watch sweaty people huffing by, but to each her own.)

She'd set up her picnic at a popular spot and was taking in the ambience, when she noticed someone at a fold-out table not far away. It was none other than Tinder Guy himself!

She texted me excitedly and asked me whether she should go and introduce herself.

'Yes!' I replied. I was equally excited for her to see, for herself, how amazing he was.

After a few hours passed with no further comms from her, I texted: 'And? How'd it go?! Isn't he amazing??'

I was ready for her to affirm everything that I had told her about him, to gush on about how truly sublime he was in every way.

Her response, though, was hardly what I was prepared for.

It comprised several photographs of him with another girl (of the Lip Gloss variety) and what appeared to be her parents.

My heart skipped a beat.

Wait, Kel, I told myself. Don't jump to conclusions.

Maybe it was his cousin or something.

He was allowed to have plans with his family on the weekend, right?

But they were awfully close...

With my thumb and index finger, I zoomed in on the pics.

Were they kissing?

With tongue??

Was that his hand on her bum?

Surely, he wouldn't be French-kissing his cousin, or fondling her little – and I mean little – bottom?

So, probably not his cousin, then.

I felt I would vomit.

It's not often that I have vehement bodily responses to emotions, but this was definitely one of those times.

My palms were slick. My heart raced. My chest tightened. I suddenly had to poo.

Classic symptoms of heartbreak, according to absolutely no medical journal ever.

I was sore.

I was so incredibly sore.

I was also in shock.

Numb might describe it, but that's not quite right because there were still feelings. But it was as though the feelings and the pain were happening outside of myself.

I felt detached from the world. From him. From our relationship.

But, mostly, I felt detached from my own sense of being.

This shock didn't last all that long.

(Thank goodness, given that it was a touch dramatic).

When it wore off, I was overcome with intense, all-consuming anger.

I hadn't been this upset over my breakup with SD – my first love, and the father of my child!

I was also absolutely mortified. I was furious, ashamed, and embarrassed.

I was so decidedly embarrassed.

I was embarrassed that I had been so naïve. I was embarrassed that I could have been so stupid to think that an evenings-only relationship could ever have amounted to anything more than sex. I was embarrassed that I had persuaded myself that keeping our relationship in a 'bubble' was something that I had wanted.

I needed to confront him, but how?

When in doubt, write an essay.

Although I was well versed in the art of essay-writing and had the ability to string together a 350 000-word letter of complaint, I somehow could not formulate an appropriate response to the newfound knowledge that I had been so well played; that I'd been taken for a fool.

Where to begin?

Also, how to articulate my aggression while maintaining decorum and being a refined lady?

Perhaps it might have been easier in person. Tone is everything.

Also, gesticulation. One hand over my heart (showing off fab manicure), teacup in the other hand.

Pinkie finger up.

Smile in my eyes.

'C.U. Never. Tinderguy.'

Ladylike.

Since I clearly could not address him in person, and my vocabulary seemed, for once, to be failing me, I forwarded him the pictures with the caption: 'This would explain the aloofness.'

No response.

'It's been real.'

No response.

'Okay, joke's on me. It hasn't been real. Nothing was real.'

No response.

'I wish you well.'

No response.

Then I had a sickening realisation. It was the realisation that, technically, he hadn't actually done anything wrong.

We'd never discussed exclusivity. He'd never promised that he wouldn't see anyone else. In fact, he'd never said that he wasn't seeing anyone else already.

And I'd never asked.

Nevertheless, something felt very wrong about the whole situation. It reeked of subterfuge. He may not theoretically have done anything wrong, but we live in a practical world where theory doesn't always pass muster. Life is a hands-on business, and his hands had apparently been very much on someone else.

On her teeny botty.

I'd caught him out.

But he wasn't the only one who'd been caught.

I'd been caught too.

Not caught out, because I'd done nothing improper.

But caught hook, line, and sinker, nonetheless.

The only thing worse than having someone cheat on you, is when you can't even be mad that they have cheated on you, because they'd never promised that they wouldn't.

I hadn't seen it coming.

I didn't even think that was a thing.

I shall pause here to hop on my pretend soapbox and explain a foundational principle of relationships.

*hops on*

So, major life lesson: apparently you need to explicitly ask your man whether he is keen to put his fancy bits into someone else's fancy bits when he is putting his fancy bits into your fancy bits.

Who knew?

I'd had no idea. I didn't realise that this was something one had to think about when one was having loads of astronomically awesome sex with someone.

In all my innocence (yeah, right), I'd not understood that exclusivity couldn't be simply assumed.

It's like a chair with no 'reserved' sign – leave it empty for two seconds, and someone else's bum will be all over it.

*hops off*

I was beside myself that my guy had gone and fucked off with someone else. And then it hit me: was he even actually my guy, or was he her guy?

I couldn't figure out whether I was the main course, or merely the side dish.

Either way, it felt like he'd stuck a knife in me.

I was hurt and broken and angry.

I felt that he had betrayed me.

But, even worse, I felt that I had betrayed myself in my pitiable naïveté.

Self-loathing began to move in faster than a fat kid towards a doughnut (and I would know about that).

How could I have missed the signs? The emotional roadblocks. The flashing detour signals. The bright red 'TURN BACK NOW!' billboard with my name on it.

The things that had initially charmed me suddenly became the things that made me realise Tinder Guy was a cad.

If his phone rang while I was around, he never answered.

He never made plans outside of his house.

He never made plans on weekends.

He never wanted to introduce me to anyone.

He never attempted to advance our relationship.

He never spoke about any kind of future.

He never committed to me exclusively.

I stewed about this. I checked my phone at least 750 million times to see if he had read my texts. When I wasn't checking my phone, I was conjuring any number of horrendous scenarios that could make sense of the shitty situation I found myself in.

His philandering had left me floundering.

I imagined him laughing at me, thinking me an absolute fool. I pictured him loving the girl in the photographs in the same intimate ways he'd loved me. I thought of them showering together. I thought about him waking her up in the middle of the night with the promise of scintillating sleepy sex.

Then it got worse. I imagined them holding hands at family braais and going on weekends away together. Him introducing her to all his friends. To his family.

The images kept popping into my mind like unsolicited social-media ads. The more I tried to ignore them, the more my psychological algorithm sent my way – each one more targeted and relentless.

Meanwhile, in the real world, he hadn't responded to my text messages in days. The ticks remained grey; my messages were unread.

I should have blocked him and deleted his number – or at the very least deleted the chat, to stop myself from obsessing over it – yet I desperately needed to know that he knew that he had been caught out, that he had hurt me, that I was aware our entire relationship had been a sham, and that my being aware meant that I wasn't as stupid as he so evidently thought I was.

Fucking coward.

I suppose I could have just phoned him. But it wasn't that kind of relationship. Also, I was afraid of what he might say.

Who's the coward now?

A few days later – when I was at work, trying desperately to concentrate through the intense headache that had set in alongside the heartache – I received a Facetime request from him.

My initial response to seeing his name light up on my phone screen was absolute disbelief.

This guy had a serious set of balls, I thought.

What business had he contacting me, after what he'd done?

The audacity!

How fucking dare he?!

My intrigue won me over, though. I simply had to answer the call to find out what he had to say for himself.

I quickly tidied my hair and pinched my pallid cheeks. Then, taking a deep breath and channelling my inner calm, I answered the Facetime call.

To my utter astonishment, he was smiling. He seemed in great spirits and looked genuinely happy to be talking to me.

Was I in the Twilight Zone?

Was this guy for real?

I remained polite. I didn't immediately bring up his 'comrade' at the marathon. I simply remarked that I'd sent him texts and that he evidently had not seen them.

He nonchalantly explained that his phone had been stolen, and that was why he was using his iPad to contact me.

Say what, now?

My heart paused, mid-beat. I felt momentarily disorientated. I thought I might fall off my ergonomic office chair.

He hadn't received my texts.

Not one.

Still channelling my inner calm, I allowed him to shoot the breeze for what felt like an eternity before telling him that my friend had seen him at the Comrades.

There was no need to elaborate further, because his upbeat, charming demeanour immediately transformed. This was indication enough for me that he knew exactly what I meant.

He went silent.

There was no excuse.

No explanation.

No apology.

There wasn't much of anything, really.

He just sat there, with a mouth full of perfectly straight teeth, and said nothing.

Awkwardness securely established, the call ended with a clipped, 'Have a good day' from my end.

Actually, I hoped he'd have a really horrible day – a day filled with copious amounts of shit. A decidedly horrible, shitty day.

After that call, I had more questions than I did answers.

Had it all been a lie?

Why had he done this to me?

How had I not noticed?

Who was this other girl?

How had I landed up in this love triangle?

Shit, what if it was a rectangle? Or pentagon?

I don't even like trigonometry.

It was a damn polygon, wasn't it?

Tell me it wasn't a fucking polygon.

For the rest of the day, I was reeling over the lack of information, explanation, or apology. I also reeled over the things that I wished I had said to him on the Facetime call. I wanted to tell him how he had made me feel. I wanted to tell him what an arsehole he was. I wanted to tell him that I never wanted to speak to him again.

Except that I did want to speak to him again.

Desperately.

I wanted to talk it through. To figure out what went wrong.

Maybe it truly was just a huge misunderstanding – some god-awful, farcical fuckup?

Spoiler alert: as it turns out, it wasn't.

He evidently got a new phone, because that evening, he texted me to say that he was 'genuinely sorry'.

Genuinely sorry for what?

For cheating?

For misleading me?

For lying about it?

For being caught?

I never specifically asked.

I simply did what any sane human would do in this situation: I accepted his apology.

Super sane.

I not only accepted the apology, but I also sent him a response to his apology, advising him that I had forgiven him.

So, so sane.

As sane as a mental patient in a lunatic asylum. Drooling down their straitjacket.

And I went on: 'If you are ever in a position to truly give our relationship a bash, I really hope you'll be in touch,' I texted him.

I was a special kind of lunatic.

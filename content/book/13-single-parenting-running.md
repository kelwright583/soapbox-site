# Running

Over the next few years, I dealt with my emotional pain by taking up a wholesome hobby. I started running.

Running out of patience.

Running out of money.

Running away from responsibility.

Running away from my hurt.

And doing all of this running without any sense of direction.

I still struggled with guilt and shame because I felt I needed to atone for my life choices. The conundrum, though, was that I could not bring myself to feel anything but elation for having had my daughter. I battled with depression, but her chubby smiles and adorable antics were the glimmers of light in my darkest days.

I grappled with regret. I regretted my decision to sleep with SD. I regretted that my innocence was lost. I regretted taking my family to the shit and having them implicated in the consequences. I regretted that, as a result of my foolishness, my daughter might face added challenges as she grew up. But there was no way I regretted bringing her into the world.

How the pickled sphinx do you show your family that you are dreadfully sorry for fucking up, and demonstrate that you are genuinely remorseful, without actually regretting the outcome of your actions?

I was running in emotional circles.

SD had contributed a total of one bag of nappies and a few presents for the birthdays which he remembered, so I still relied heavily on my folks for financial support. This created a cocktail of inadequacy, guilt, and fuckery. (Only, this cocktail didn't have a pretty paper umbrella, and it left a taste in my mouth worse than tequila.)

It grated me; I felt strongly at the time that, to deem myself a fully-fledged mum, I needed to provide for my daughter without assistance.

Unfortunately, the fog of depression blurred my vision for what could have been a good challenge. On one hand, I was so keen to prove to everyone that I could be an exemplary mum, in spite of my age. I wanted to take care of my child. Her and me against the world, and all that. And yet, on the other hand, when reminded of every area in which I fell short, the feeling that someone else might be better suited for the position of parent weighed heavily on me.

Depression, therefore, was intricately interwoven with self-loathing, and it became difficult to identify whether one was the catalyst for the other, and if so, which came first. It was the epitome of a vicious cycle: the more I felt I couldn't do it, or the more I felt she deserved a better parent, a better lifestyle, a mum who wouldn't be so jolly pathetic, the less I could actually do anything, and the more depressed I became.

I was still running round and round in my head, and now I was starting to get dizzy.

I managed to persuade myself that my absence was better for her than my presence. With this deduction – which was formulated on the foundations of martyrdom – I quickly became the most selfish, self-seeking, inwardly focused cop-out there ever was.

This meant that it took much longer than it should have to bond with my daughter. The more I hated myself, the more I held my distance.

I took up the role of socialite with outward confidence. I did what every 20-something (without children) did. I went to clubs in the evenings, drank the night away, and then slept off the debauchery the next day.

Wash.

Rinse.

Repeat.

Any primal instinct I had for providing and nurturing had clearly missed the memo –

or hit the snooze button. Which is what I did most days, as a result of the night before.

When I ran out of drinking money, I got a waitressing job (I was by then studying toward my degree, and needed shift work that I could schedule around my lecture timetable).

Sadly, working for a fish restaurant made me very little money and left me smelling – on a good day – like hake and fried sick. I soon discovered that pubs were a much quicker way to make moolah, especially if one donned a teeny pair of shorts and a tight-fitting top, and ensured that the patrons got so incredibly drunk that they insisted that you calculate the tip for them. (While my moral compass may have been bust, my onboard calculator, patently, was not).

Making money in pubs was an evening affair. I would do my hair and makeup and fake tan (because tanned fat looks better than pale fat) and I would be gone from four in the afternoon and only return at around six the following morning. I spent the whole night on my feet, navigating groping hands, broken beer bottles, bar brawls, and bitch fights.

I suppose it was easy to fall into poor habits, because when my parents and child were fast asleep and only expected me home at six, there was no reason to report back before then.

So, I regularly stayed out long after my shift had finished, partying with friends (or with people I had just met) all over Durban.

There were more times than I'd care to admit when I drove home drunk – at least I had my license by then! – or when I befriended naughty humans, and there were plenty of times I stayed out dancing until the lights came back on in the clubs. Fortunately, I wasn't pulled out of a crack house or jailed for public indecency (I have always been very good about being indecent in private).

Perpetually on the run from reality, I was being selfish and pathetic.

And there was only so much drinking, dancing, and general misconduct that I could endure before it became meaningless and void of any satisfaction (especially when I had a beautiful little human waiting for me at home). Doing shitty things and avoiding responsibility were not only self-serving, but they made me feel incredibly lonely.

There was, eventually, a turning point. It wasn't a massive, crash-and-burn, near-death experience which made me sit up and take notice. I don't think everyone needs to hit 'rock bottom' in order to make a change. It certainly wasn't the case for me.

One day I was whining to my mum, for the umpteenth time, that I wanted to drive myself off a cliff.

My mum – evidently also reaching the end of her tether – pulled out her own version of a soapbox and responded sonorously, 'Since I paid good money for that car, how about, instead of driving yourself off a cliff, you drive yourself to the cliff, park the car, and then merely throw yourself off? Alternatively, you can choose to believe that you are precious, and keep working at it.'

What choice did I have? It seemed an awful inconvenience to have to get out of the car and actually hurl myself down the cliff. And what if I couldn't find parking?

Also, the soapbox was my thing, not hers!

So, I kept working at it.

One day at a time.

One nap, one meal, one bath at a time.

Baby steps, as they say.

I slowed the frenzied running down to a jog, then a walk, then individual, conscious steps.

In fact, as my daughter grew before my eyes and began take her first wobbly toddles across the living room, I found that I was equally unsteady in finding my feet as a parent, but I kept at it with the same gritty determination. It must be genetic.

There were days when I did really well and felt as though I was on top of things. She went down for naps easily. I fed her nutritious lunches. There was no crying (from her or from me).

Then there were days when things came crashing down like the pots and pans she threw around the kitchen, and routines went down the toilet faster than my new mascara (which my daughter had dropped in there, along with an entire roll of toilet paper, my hairbrush, and her lunchtime snack).

With time, though, the good days began to outnumber the bad.

We dealt with issues as they arose.

Potty training was easy-peasy-lemon-squeezy, because I refused to even try until she literally begged to move out of nappies. Getting rid of her dummy was a walk in the park, since she bit holes in every last one, and I just never replaced them.

In time, she learnt to eat whatever I put in front of her (including the remnants of the nipples on her milk bottles, if I didn't manage to pry her pudgy hands off them in time), and to sleep through most nights.

Not that this made much tangible difference to me – my sleep was still wrecked by the 47 times a night I had to get up to wee, and the trying to solve the world's food scarcity problems, global warming, and other big issues at 3 am.

Finally, we began to bond, my daughter and me. And I realised that she needed me. She chose me. Even when I sucked at consoling her tantrums, she still wanted me to do it. Before, when I had staggered into the house at 6 am, flaunting varying degrees of intoxication, she still chose to give me kisses before falling promptly back asleep. Now, when I stumbled over the words in her illustrated book of fairy tales, she still wanted me to read her stories. One of her favourites was 'The Ugly Duckling'.

She made me do sound effects.

Quack.

I may not have been a model mum, but I was her mum, and it became more apparent over time that, despite my misgivings about parenthood, I actually wanted her to keep choosing me.

Ooh, this might be a soapbox moment!

*ahem, ahem*

I am well aware there are plenty of mums who would outshine me every single day of the week, and twice on Saturdays.

On Saturdays, I don't even wear a bra or pants, and I do nothing but binge-watch series and binge-eat junk food.

Some Saturdays, I brush my hair.

Actually, no. I don't do that, either.

Fine.

Um...

I brush my teeth.

Yes, I brush my teeth on Saturdays.

Well, some Saturdays.

I know that, if something were to happen to me, there would be plenty of people who could take care of my child, and do a damn good job of it.

Plenty of people who wear pants on Saturdays.

Come to think of it, I don't imagine I should be on a soapbox if I am not wearing pants.

*steps off soapbox to go and find pants*

My mum had reminded me that I was precious, and my kid had convinced me of my worth. She convinced me that I needed to choose me too. Even if I didn't always wear pants.

In a storybook twist of poetic justice, I found was enacting my own ugly duckling story: when I realised that I was wired toward being a swan, I no longer felt I needed to masquerade as a duck.

*locates pants*

As it turned out, being a swan was far easier than not giving a duck.

# Distance

Upon touchdown back in Durban, I sent him a text saying that I'd landed safely and thanking him for all he had done to make our time together so special.

His response: 'Pleasure. Enjoy the rest of your week.'

'You too. Have a wonderful w...'

Wait.

What?

'Enjoy the rest of your week'???

WTF?

You invite me into your home, you ask my opinion on your décor, you wine and dine me on epically romantic dates, you take me to bed, you sleep with your arm around me for a fortnight, and now I must 'enjoy my week'?

What.

The.

Actual.

Fuck?

Wait, Kel, calm your knickers, I told myself.

This was just him being overly formal. That whole well-brought-up-Afrikaans-boy thing. It's actually very sweet.

Right?

Only, he hadn't been formal with me for months – not since the early days of the insurance claims. Not since he'd started using my private number. Not since he'd given me an adorable pet name.

Something smelt suspiciously like 'you've got to be fucking kidding me'.

As I watched the other passengers filing down the centre aisle, a strange sadness fell over me, like a drunk fucktard in a pub at closing time.

Yet, ever the optimist, I shrugged it off in much the same way I would deal with the fucktard: politely but firmly, before putting as much distance between me and the sadness as possible.

My mum fetched me from the airport. She was in great spirits and very excited to get the recital of all that I'd gotten up to during my time away. She even asked whether I would be heading back for Christmas, which was then just a few weeks away. I rehashed the highlights of the holiday with a smile because I couldn't bear to expose the turmoil I was suddenly feeling inside.

This turmoil was partly indigestion (even after the fart, without having had an actual poo in two weeks, I was still suffering). But my heart was in turmoil too. My chest felt tight, my heartbeat restricted.

The good news was that seeing my daughter after the longest time I'd ever been without her was totally the best thing in the world.

Good gracious, I'd missed that kid!

Her hug when I got inside the house felt like the best kind of comfy blanket. She took the edge off the unsettledness.

In the weeks that followed, I did the school run, the work run, and all the run of the mill things (never doing any actual running, of course).

There wasn't much contact with the Afrikaner.

I was confused about how to handle this. I wanted to talk to him, but couldn't find anything to say that was topical, light-hearted, and low-pressure.

'Just to let you know, I'm not thinking of you much, only like a little bit. Sort of. Haha.'

Nope, that certainly wasn't going to work.

'Oh my gosh, it is really hot here in KwaZulu-Natal. My favourite topic is the weather.'

Relationship winner over here.

On his birthday, I couriered a gift to his office. Christmas Day, I sent warm wishes. A week later, I wished him Happy New Year. In return: silence.

That was it.

He didn't call.

He didn't text.

It seemed he was determined to put distance between us.

You know, as if 2 000 kilometres wasn't enough.

Life gets busy, I told myself.

It's not always convenient to call or text. I understood that he was a successful businessman, that he had good, wholesome hobbies, and that he maintained a close relationship with his family. So, it seemed perfectly plausible that he was simply busy and therefore couldn't chat as frequently.

Very plausible.

No?

Come to think of it, he hadn't introduced me to his family. Over the months, he'd regularly spoken about his family's legendary Sunday lunches, but he'd skipped these during my visit. Why was that?

I tried to ignore these uncomfortable feelings. I didn't want to bombard him with my insecurities.

Also, I'd exhausted all my emotional funds, so had no intention of purchasing a ticket to board the Crazy Train.

But I couldn't stop thinking (okay, fine, obsessing) about him. Every minute of every day, I ruminated. In the mornings, I pictured him with his dog, and his banana. During the day, I wondered what he was doing, what he was eating for lunch, how his redecorating was going, what was happening in his business (and if I would be getting a call from him about his insurance... or anything else). In the evenings, I thought of his crossed legs as he relaxed in the lounge. At night, I thought about him turning over awkwardly in bed.

Without me there.

I'd grown feelings for him that were – evidently, given his distance and subsequent detachment – misplaced.

That was the problem.

I loved him.

I hadn't told him I loved him, of course.

You must never say 'I love you' first.

Say things like 'Lots of love.'

You can throw in an artful 'and light' at the end of this.

Maybe even a 'yo' to spice things up.

This way you can pretend that you are on fleek.

'Lots of love and light, yo.'

You must use some cool, ghetto hand gesture or emoji when you say 'yo'.

On second thought, don't do this. You'll sound like a rapper.

Who is named after money. The kind of money that sits in the middle compartment of your purse, and frequently threatens to break the zip.

Ridiculous.

But it didn't matter if I loved him, because at this point, I wasn't even getting an 'I miss you'.

I started to recalibrate. I told myself I'd survived worse. That if he wasn't choosing me, I wouldn't beg. That I deserved more than silence.

A few weeks later – after the Christmas to which I had not been invited – he texted. My heart skipped a beat (in the good way).

His message asked if we could 'chat'. My heart skipped another beat (in the bad way).

He said that he wasn't sure that a committed relationship was what he wanted right then. That he was keen to 'take it slow', 'take the pressure off', and 'see where things went'.

Alarm bells started clanging, and everything inside me screamed 'Not again!'

But I was so certain of the Afrikaner's maturity, his wisdom, his class, and his other obvious differences to Tinder Guy, that I persuaded myself that this time it would be different. The Afrikaner was a gentleman. He wouldn't toy with my heart.

I even asked him outright whether his change of mind was because he was keen to see other people. He fiercely denied this. He said he simply wanted to take things slow.

Because I didn't know the status of our relationship (could it even be called a relationship?), I had no idea how to conduct myself. Should I have pursued him to show him that I cared? Should I have left him alone? Should I have played hard-to-get? A guy once asked me whether I played hard-to-get. I can't remember my exact response, but it certainly wasn't, 'Yes, I frequently play games with other people's emotions.'

What is expected of a girl in this situation?

Surprise, surprise – I have some thoughts on the matter.

*stomps with reckless abandon on soapbox*

On the one hand the world says: 'Seize the day!'

'Go get 'em!'

'If you want something, don't take no for an answer!'

'Carpe diem!' (If you're really pretentious.)

But, on the other hand, we are also told: 'If they love you, they will come to you.'

'If it is meant to be, it will happen.'

'You can't force fate.'

'Que sera sera.' (If you're Doris Day.)

Which one is it?!

Must I go get him? Or should I wait for him to come get me?

If I wait in a relatively obvious spot, will that make it easier for him to find me? Like when kids play hide-and-seek and someone hides behind the curtain, a discernible bulge evidencing their position. (That was always me. I was never dextrous, creative, or fast enough to find a better hiding place. Would that proclivity stand me in good stead as an adult negotiating a love affair?)

What the spilled starlight does 'take it slow' even mean? How do you define slow? Einstein said that time is relative. So, when the Afrikaner said 'slow', what exactly did he have in mind? Slow relative to what? To a shotgun wedding? To continental drift?

Then he (the Afrikaner, not Einstein – just for clarity) sent me a link to some fatuous YouTube video about 'creating space for someone to close the gap'. What twisted, cryptic garbage was this, and who had the PhD in incomprehensible nonsense required to make sense of it?

It was completely contradictory – how do you make space and, at the same time, diminish space?

And how the fuck do you do this in relative time?

I clearly needed to brush up on my understanding of the space-time continuum. Who knew that was a prerequisite in romantic relationships?

I gave it some more thought.

Perhaps 'creating space to close the gap' was supposed to mean creating space in order to give someone a running start, to build up their courage to end things?

Or to give them space to realise that they wanted you, so that they would come back to you?

Was I supposed to be the one creating the space for him to chase me?

Was he creating space for me to chase him?

Why the fuck are we chasing each other, anyway?

Why is this a game?

This is bullshit, I thought. It was like building IKEA furniture using a Swedish guide, when you only speak English.

And swear in Afrikaans.

Fok.

Getting off my soapbox, I thought I might embark on objective introspection.

Perhaps he was trying to communicate with me in a way that didn't hurt or offend?

Maybe?

Maybe he was trying to say how he felt, but was too scared to call a spade a spade.

Instead, he called it an implement consisting of a broad blade or scoop attached to a long handle used for moving or removing loose matter from the ground. Nice and clear, right? Now I knew exactly what he meant about the state of our relationship.

I began to fret about the situation.

I began to question everything. I was no longer convinced that he ever really wanted kids. He probably didn't want me to move to Cape Town. He was probably just saying things he thought I wanted to hear because he was too afraid to be frank.

Truthfully, I didn't particularly want to move either, or have more kids, or speak in Afrikaans, but I was taking my cues from him. I was prepared to do all of those things – for him.

Before long, I began to lose confidence in who I was and the contentment that I had started to feel. My focus became solely on what I could do to win him back.

Sound familiar?

I understood that my insecurity could get in the way. But then, when I looked back at the times in my life when I'd felt most insecure, it turned out that I was actually right to have questioned the circumstances.

So, how insecure was I, really?

Perceptive.

I was perceptive.

Or was I?

Apparently, I could find the emotional funds for the Crazy Train ticket after all (lying in the middle compartment of my falling-apart purse).

I fell, once again, into the trap of codependence. I needed so desperately to be validated and to have my love returned, and so, actually, his neglect only really served to fuel me on.

'Rock, meet Hard Place. Hard Place, Rock.'

Here we go again.

I found it increasingly difficult to manage my expectations, and even more difficult to navigate his.

I didn't expect him to give up his lifestyle and move to KZN.

I didn't need the quaint, #bespoke holidays. I didn't need the fancy restaurants or extravagant touches.

I mean, I could totally get behind that, though.

If needed.

I didn't care about his sizeable assets (with one – particularly sizeable – exception).

I didn't expect him to be emotionally so astute that he understood all Five Love Languages, the full spectrum of human emotion, and all the feels (although being proficient in articulating some of the feels might have been helpful).

My expectations stretched no further than the famous lyrics sung by Aretha Franklin:

R.

E.

S.

P.

E.

C.

T.

Just some basic manners, for crying out loud (or singing out loud).

I expected him to be truthful. And I had the distinct feeling that he wasn't doing that.

At some point, he'd obviously changed his mind. He must have realised that what – or whom – he wanted was not me. In hindsight, he probably wanted to end whatever it was that we had going, but was too afraid to say so.

Had he been hinting?

Had I missed the metaphorical memo?

Was he thinking, 'Why is this domkop not getting the message?'

Actually, FFS, bring me back my soapbox.

Men, stop with the bloody hints already.

You hint when you want a blowy before sex.

You don't hint when someone is falling for you, and when you have zero intention of catching them before they plunge face-first into a cesspool of devastation.

I understood that if someone genuinely wanted me in their life, they'd make a plan to be in mine. He clearly didn't want me in his life that badly.

But if he knew this, why didn't he say so?

While we're relying on pop culture's vocal artists to articulate the complexities of human relationships, Kenny Rogers has some solid life advice:

'Know when to walk away. Know when to run.'

Personally, I always choose to walk briskly. You know I don't run.

Unless something is chasing me, maybe. Like a lion.

Or a stampede.

I will run if there is a stampede of lions chasing me.

I will run with all the dignity and respect that a fatty with podgy legs and jiggly thighs can muster.

Meanwhile, my daughter and my folks were eager to know the status of my new relationship. I'd been playing it coy, telling them that it was 'still early days' and that we would 'see what happened'.

Because how the heck could I tell them that the status of our relationship took a nosedive after he met me? (Remember, they didn't know that we'd met before.)

How could I tell them that, after getting to know me, he decided he didn't like me?

I didn't even poo at his house.

What's not to like?

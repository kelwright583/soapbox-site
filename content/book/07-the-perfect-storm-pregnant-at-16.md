# Pregnant at 16

Sixteen.

I was 16 and pregnant.

My world had changed in an instant.

One moment, I was doing adult things in bed, and the next moment, I was having to lie in it.

Go figure.

Having fallen pregnant, I felt like I'd lost my place in life.

I'd lost my role in the family because 16-year-old daughters aren't supposed to fall pregnant. I'd lost my place as a friend because my life no longer resembled my peers'. I'd lost my place as a student because the school shunned me. I'd lost my ability to dream about a future. I'd lost my plans to go overseas to study (okay, fine, au pair).

I'd lost station, credibility, and inclusivity because, within a flash, I no longer had a thing in common with anyone.

In only six months, I gained a child and lost everything else.

I am aware that the human gestation period is nine months.

However, I only learnt of my pregnancy three months in, because I was still young enough that my period had not settled into a regular cycle and so it was hard to keep track.

Okay, you got me!

That is a complete untruth.

My pregnancy felt as though it was only six months because for two months of the first trimester, I was in denial, and the other month I was putting together my alibi.

As it turned out, trying to persuade my parents that I am basically just Mary, and that the new Jesus was baking inside my virgin oven, was not as convincing as I'd thought.

They didn't believe the bit about falling pregnant during my 'first time', either.

Being pregnant as a teenager was hard, but in ways one might not expect.

Sure, having to hide the bump was difficult. Not being able to do normal, medium-risk physical activities like ice skating or hiking or jolling with friends was not so wonderful. Having to grow up overnight was tough.

But more than the obvious things, what I found most difficult was that what should (eventually) have been the most natural culmination and achievement of life was the very thing that had me isolated and labelled as 'stupid', 'careless', 'second-rate', and 'juvenile'. At least, I'm pretty sure those were the words used behind my back.

Where other (older) people were congratulated for their efforts in the manifestation of human life, I was cursed for my poor decision-making.

Where other women celebrated the start of motherhood, I was cast as a cautionary tale.

In different circumstances, the same situation would have earned me balloons and baby showers.

Not brokenness and 'bumpy' beginnings.

I left school temporarily in my matric year, when I was no longer able to pass the weekly school uniform inspections. These were conducted by the prefects, who took their role as enforcers of dress code ultra-seriously. When my skirt could no longer button up, when my oversized school jersey could no longer hide my growing tummy, when my school top could no longer be fastened over my boobs, and when the matric enforcers no longer found it appealing that at least some parts of me were determined not to shrink from purpose, I was compelled to continue with the syllabus at home.

I only did the bare minimum that counted for marks. The rest of the time I spent binge-watching TV and indulging my craving for muesli – which made me overwhelmingly nauseous and induced severe indigestion.

That didn't stop me from eating a bag a day.

A quitter, I am not.

This nausea continued throughout my pregnancy, and not just in the early part of the day.

'Morning sickness', my ever-expanding botty.

I was sick the entire day. Every day.

I found that if I started the day eating things like dry crackers and biscuits, and then ended the day eating dry crackers and biscuits, and then also ate crackers and biscuits, like, throughout the day (in between all the muesli), then the nausea was manageable. It was only really bad if my mouth wasn't moving. The easiest solution was to masticate at all hours.

This is not to be confused with masturbating at all hours.

Although some women experience bouts of horniness during pregnancy, masturbating is still – even amidst #woke society – frowned upon, when done in public.

Although, truthfully, I didn't really go out in public because this interfered with binge-watching and binge-eating.

Being in public with me also completely humiliated my folks.

I read as much as I could on the things that I should expect whilst expecting, but so often the books were insufficient. I had so many questions which I felt I couldn't ask because – although my folks were now fully on board and were determined to offer their utmost support – I worried that they were only supporting me as an act of obligation.

I felt like I had disappointed everyone.

I felt that my pregnancy was a taboo topic, even though I desperately wanted to talk about it.

I wanted to show my excitement, to buy parenting books, debate birthing options, and coo over cute baby clothing and those tiny, tiny shoes. I wanted to count down the days until I met my child. I wanted to prove that he or she was loved and wanted, even if not planned.

I felt like I owed my child that much.

But I worried that showing anything other than emotional turmoil would make it seem like I had not realised the severity of my actions. I was aware that if I didn't display shame and remorse, people would think that I didn't care that I had cocked up so significantly and that everyone nearest to me had to pay the price.

And the price was steep.

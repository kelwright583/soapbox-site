# McDonald's for Dinner

My daughter went off to 'big school' when she was five. I opted to send her to a private school, because I have a very specific fondness of seeing just how fine I can cut it with finances and of making my life harder than it needs to be.

Goodness, if you ever want to feel inadequate, be a single, 20-something mum in a sea of married mid-30-year-olds. Then drive your little red Noddy car into the throng of luxury SUVs in the private school parking lot.

One of the #stayathomemoms asked me if I could au pair for her child as well.

#winning.

All my inadequacy issues weren't great for my mental health, but then that was nothing new. Perhaps it was just my neurotic personality, or my life choices, or poor luck, or the prohibitive cost of therapy.

I also don't know that I ever truly recovered from postnatal depression.

Although, by now it's probably just plain depression.

Straight up, simple, entry-level misery.

But it's fine, because I choose no longer to identify as depressed.

I'm like those people who smoke vapes and e-cigarettes, and who don't identify as smokers.

Insurance broker: 'Are you a smoker?'

Through fits of phlegmy coughing: 'Oh, God, no!'

Similarly:

'Are you depressed?'

'Well, most days I want to live – except on the days that I want to die, like those days between the third of the month and pay day. But no, not depressed at all.'

Luckily, my depression is a complete non-issue as long as I am taking a cocktail of Schedule 6 drugs.

Jokes.

Kind of.

But a sneaky tablet every day certainly helps.

Depression or no, I would like to think that I am navigating parenting with the same level of finesse that I am navigating my 30s.

I started off at the bottom, and now I am slightly above the bottom.

I've progressed from my little Noddy car to a regular, reliable, very plain vehicle. (Unfortunately, it can't mount pavements.)

I confess that, as I progressed up the childrearing ladder, that didn't mean I left parental stuff-ups behind me – they just got fancier. For example, I forgot to be the tooth fairy on two occasions.

Now, there's a story (or two). This would be a good time to pull out that soapbox.

Ladies and gentlemen, why in the gay goblin's ears must kids make it so damn difficult for us to fulfil their delusional dental fantasies? If they truly want us to deliver on their tooth-related fairytales, the least they could do would be to put the tooth in an easily accessible spot on the bedside table, with a sign saying, 'TOOTH HERE'.

It's as if they purposefully set us up for failure.

'Help me to help you, for fuck's sake!'

Also: 'Cry in your heart, like a winner. I feel horrendous enough already, without you alerting the entire house that I am the queen of all fuckups.'

But back to the story...

The first time, I got off on a technicality. When my daughter woke up to find her tooth still under her pillow instead of cold, hard cash, she broke into machine-gun cries of disappointment.

To rectify the life-and-death situation, I grabbed the only money I had in my purse (a R100 note), scrunched it into my palm, and – on hands and knees – pretended to 'look' for it. While she was distracted smearing snot into her sleeve, I threw the cash under her bed and exclaimed, 'Oh, there it is! It must have dropped on the floor.'

When she queried why the tooth had not been taken, I persuaded her that fairies, fragile as they are, are unable to manoeuvre themselves so far under a pillow, and that the tooth fairy probably just wanted to avoid ripping her wings off.

Technicality – boom!

Although I have never played poker (you know, Christian upbringing), my bluff game is unmatched – so much so that Aaron Korsh once offered me a job.

(That's another bluff.)

The second time I forgot to be the tooth fairy, I couldn't lie my way out of it. I just succumbed to the failure, gave up, and waved the proverbial white flag.

Then I had to wave a R100 note.

One hundred fucking Rand.

For a tooth.

Since then, I have been paying for her every dental deliverance, and the price has increased on the basis that she now knows my true identity and hangs it over my head like a Mexican drug lord.

I tried to make up for my parenting mishaps with my eagerness to improve. In fact, the leading survival tactic in my childrearing arsenal (which was mostly just arse) was enthusiasm. I was so keen to do what was right by my child that I spent hours researching a thousand ways to make sure she would turn out nothing short of extraordinary. I set up routines that not even I was capable of sticking to. I planned creative days, prepared star charts, drew up meal plans, and then succumbed to 'Oh, fuck it, have the blinking two-minute noodles ten minutes before dinner, if that's what you want!'

No one can maintain enthusiasm perpetually.

I lost my shit on more occasions than I would like to admit.

Sometimes I worried that the only legacy I would leave my child would be a list of resourceful profanities. 'Wank-spanner', 'thundering walloper', 'knob-swallow', and 'roaring twat-waffle' are but a few of the phrases she has had to endure.

Not directed at her! Good gravy – I am not entirely derailed.

No, this vocab tended to feature when I'd forgotten that she was in the car with me, because I'd temporarily gone off the reservation, thinking about the hundred things I needed to achieve that day. I would then let loose on a taxi driver stopping to pick up passengers in the middle of a bloody highway. Letting loose from behind the safety of my closed windows, where he couldn't hear me.

#bebrave

Another area about which I was enthusiastic was paediatric nutrition. I was particularly inspired by those women on Pinterest who cut their kids' sarmies into dinosaurs and pressed their boiled eggs into little panda-shaped moulds.

That enthusiasm lasted exactly as long as it took me to realise that I didn't want to spend my hard-earned money on overpriced, novelty egg moulds designed to turn lunch into art and parents into suckers. Nice try, Pinterest Mom, but I'm not about to blow my bucks on enriching your back-to-school side hustle.

So, that inspiration stayed on my Pinboard.

Frankly, enthusiasm can only take one so far. But enthusiasm and McDonald's can get one further than one might expect.

On most days, I could just about manage:

Reduced-sugar cereal for breakfast.

A (regular-shaped) sarmie and a piece of fruit for lunch. Maybe chuck in the odd pretzel or a sweetie, if I was feeling lavish.

Meat and three veg for dinner.

Most days.

On other days, I threw out the handbook. Tapped into my adventurous side. Planned an expedition into the wilderness.

Luckily, McDonald's is everywhere. Even in the wilderness. So, my kid could still get her meat and three veg. Only, this time, in the form of a burger (with pickled cucumber, fried onion, and tomato sauce – can't you count?).

And fries.

And a sugar-free Coke.

Did you honestly think I would give my kid full-sugar Coke?

What do you think I am – some kind of sociopathic, semi-literate simpleton?

On balance, in spite of every parenting faux pas that I gloriously accomplished, I am convinced that I deserve props for my all-consuming love for my daughter. (Even if she never got panda-shaped boiled eggs in her lunchbox.)

And for keeping her alive all this time.

And because she, at least, wears pants on Saturdays.

Also, because I say things like 'sociopathic, semi-literate simpleton'.

But mostly the all-consuming love.

At the end of the day, that's all we can bank on, isn't it?

Love.

Enthusiasm.

And McDonald's.

Universal values.

You, too, deserve immense props for loving your kid as hard as you do.

Keep that up, you fabulous kid-lover, you.

Yikes.

That sounds horrendous.

Let's try that again:

Keep that up, you fabulous, loving parent, you!

Turning Thirty and Other Acts of Defiance

8 min read

I turned thirty in a pandemic, alone, with a toddler who'd just discovered the word "no" and a cat who'd discovered the joy of knocking things off shelves. It was less a celebration and more a survival milestone. My daughter woke me that morning by sitting on my face, which I've come to understand is her primary love language. My cat knocked my phone off the bedside table approximately forty seconds later. Happy birthday to me.

Thirty is supposed to feel like something. The cultural agreement is that your twenties are for figuring it out and your thirties are for having figured it. There are supposed to be investments, or at least a clear investment strategy. There is supposed to be a skin routine. The skin routine is very important, apparently. I did not have a skin routine. I had a Clicks Dove moisturiser that I used when I remembered, and I was supposed to be grateful it still worked given my general approach to self-maintenance.

Instead, what I had at thirty was a toddler I'd had alone since before she could talk, a job that paid the rent without any particular ceremony, a personality that was still very much under construction, and an acute awareness that I had spent the previous decade making decisions that felt reasonable at the time and looked increasingly chaotic in hindsight. I was not where the plan said I should be. I was also starting to suspect the plan had been written by someone who had no idea what I was actually like.

Durban in lockdown is not picturesque. We are not a city that becomes more beautiful when everyone stays home. We are a city of N2 traffic and beach access and braais on Saturday and Woolworths on Sunday, and all of that was gone. My birthday was marked by a single candle in a cupcake I made from a box, because I was not going to attempt an actual cake - not for an audience of one toddler who would be more interested in the frosting than anything I'd feel about the milestone. I ate the cupcake at 8am because she wanted to know when the candle was going to happen and she had the persistence of a labour negotiator.

What I didn't expect - because I was so busy being disappointed about what thirty wasn't - was that thirty was actually fine. Not fine in the managed, "I've processed this" way. Fine in the animal, embodied way. I felt, for the first time in a very long time, like I was actually inside my own life rather than watching it happen to someone who looked like me. The pandemic stripped out the noise. I couldn't perform being fine. I couldn't be anywhere except where I was. And where I was - this chaotic, underfunded, under-slept life - was mine.

My daughter, who was two, was spectacularly uninterested in the existential dimension of my birthday. She wanted to know if the cupcake had sprinkles (it did not) and whether the cat would eat some (he would not; he had standards). She wanted to watch the same episode of Bluey for the fourth time that morning, which, fine, because Bandit Heeler is a better parent than most actual humans I know and I was taking notes.

I did not run a half marathon that day or say something wise about getting older. I did not post a reflective caption with a throwback photo. I watched Bluey, I ate a cupcake at 8am, and at some point in the afternoon my toddler climbed into my lap with a picture book about animals and fell asleep there, and I sat with her weight on me and thought: this is the actual thing. Not the skin routine or the investment account or the decade arc. This small, impractical, frequently exhausting thing.

Thirty is the year I stopped trying to be a previous version of myself and started being whoever the current version was, with whatever that version had to work with. It wasn't a revelation. It wasn't even a decision, exactly. It was more like a very quiet, slightly anticlimactic ceasefire. The war between who I thought I should be and who I actually was had been going on for most of my twenties and it ended not with a speech but with a Bluey episode and a child asleep in my lap and a cat who had knocked the remote out of reach and I couldn't be bothered to retrieve it.

I don't have it figured out. I'm not sure I ever will. Turns out that might be the whole point.

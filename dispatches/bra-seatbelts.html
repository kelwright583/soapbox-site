On Woolworths Bras and Seatbelts Disguised as Straps

6 min read

Thirty is the year your lingerie stops being flirtation and starts being infrastructure. I know this sounds dramatic. I also know it is entirely accurate. The day I stood in the Woolworths fitting room, holding a bra the approximate size and structural complexity of a small bridge, and thought this makes complete sense - that was the day I understood I had crossed a threshold.

There's a particular kind of grief that nobody prepares you for, and it's not losing a relationship or a job or a person you loved. It's the moment you buy a bra not because it's pretty but because it works. Because it has the kind of underwire that means business. Because the straps are wide enough to distribute weight meaningfully rather than decoratively. Because it comes in beige and you are fine with that.

I am not, for the record, talking about size. I am talking about function. I know women of every size who've made this transition. It's not a body thing. It's a priorities thing. The moment you start evaluating lingerie the way you evaluate a good tyre - for load-bearing capacity and reliability under pressure - you have arrived somewhere. I won't call it a destination. I'll call it a phase of life.

My twenties were spent in bras that matched. That had little bows. That occasionally had underwire that poked into my ribcage and I just... accepted that. As though suffering for aesthetics was a reasonable way to spend the decade. I once wore a strapless bra to a wedding that spent the entire reception attempting to migrate south, and I spent the entire reception attempting to stop it, and I smiled through all of it as if nothing was happening. This is what we do. This is what we did.

At some point - I genuinely cannot tell you when, it wasn't a moment so much as a slow accumulation - I stopped doing that. I started buying bras from the Woolworths section that has words like "support" and "comfort" and "full coverage" on the packaging. I started buying bras that do not require me to think about them once they are on. I started buying bras that, if I'm honest, are essentially load-bearing garments, and I have never - not once - looked back.

The thing nobody tells you is how good it feels. Not in a rebellious way. Not in a "I've freed myself from beauty standards" way, because I haven't particularly done that and I'm not going to pretend I have. But in a purely practical, deeply satisfying way. There is something genuinely luxurious about a bra that holds everything where it's supposed to be without any input from you. That you can forget you're wearing.

Woolworths understood this assignment. I want to be clear that this is not a sponsored post (I wish). But there is a particular bra - you know the one, the cotton one with the wider-than-usual straps, the ones that feel like they were designed by someone who has actually worn a bra before - that I have bought in four colours and I will continue buying until they discontinue it, at which point I will stage a quiet, dignified protest involving a strongly worded letter.

The younger women in my life sometimes look at what I'm wearing and I can see the flicker of something cross their faces. Not quite pity. Maybe something adjacent to gentle concern. I understand. I was them. I also know what I know now, which is that the flopped brownie - the practical, unglamorous, actually-does-the-job bra - is better than the real deal. The real deal has lace and straps that leave welts and a price tag that assumes you are buying it to be seen. The flopped brownie can be worn for twelve hours and you won't think about it once.

That's the whole point. Eventually, you stop wearing things that require you to manage them. You start wearing things that work. And if that means the Woolworths fitting room rather than the lingerie section of somewhere with mood lighting and a French name - well. There are worse places to arrive.

About the author
What this is

A memoir about bodies, bad boys, and the long, unglamorous business of figuring yourself out.

It starts at sixteen.

Pregnant.
Unplanned.
In possession of approximately zero useful skills - including, apparently, the ability to correctly deploy a condom.

It ends at 30(ish).

20(ish) kilograms heavier.
Two relationships worse for wear.
One daughter raised mostly well (the jury's still out on the dental hygiene).
A genuinely alarming amount of self-knowledge that was predominantly the byproduct of having made so many mistakes, and choosing to fok voort regardless.

In between:

Tinder.
Dumpster fires.
A labiaplasty that was, in hindsight, not the relationship fix I was looking for (who could have known?).
Woolworths bras that stopped being lingerie and became load-bearing infrastructure.
A solo trip to the Drakensberg to Find Myself, during which I found mostly the pits of my despair and four days of rain.
Open relationships, which are neither open nor relationships.
And a long, spectacular, recurring inability to run - metaphorically or literally.

It is also, and perhaps more than anything else, a book about a daughter. A forgiving, gracious human, who arrived before I was ready, grew up while I was still figuring out how to, and somehow - through all of it - managed to teach me more about grace, grit, and getting back up than I ever managed to teach myself.

We took our first wobbly steps at roughly the same time. Hers were cuter. Mine took considerably longer.

This is not a book about having it figured out. It is a book about what happens when you don't - and about the very specific kind of person who, in the absence of an actual soapbox, decides to write it all down anyway.

Dedication

For my daughter

You are my undoing
The shattering of every brittle belief
I ever held about myself

Where I fractured, you flourished
Where I flailed, you forgave
You turned my brokenness into bravery
You taught me grace simply by being gracious
And without ever raising your voice
You gave me mine

You changed my story
Revealed my truth
And made me want to grow
Into someone worthy of yours

The Structure

How it's organised. More or less.

Each section has a name. Each name is, technically, a category of disaster. Together they form something that looks suspiciously like a life well examined.

I
The Perfect Storm That Is Teenage Pregnancy and Motherhood

Sixteen. Pregnant. No plan. A school uniform, a blood test, and doctors who spoke about her in the third person while she sat right there. The sperm donor who went overseas. A congregation with opinions. Postnatal depression nobody asked about.

"One moment, I was doing adult things in bed. The next moment, I was having to lie in it."
II
The Shit Show That Is Single Parenting

Running out of patience, money, and direction. A red Noddy car in a private school parking lot. McDonald's for dinner. The mother who said she was precious when she couldn't believe it herself.

"At the end of the day, that's all we can bank on, isn't it? Love. Enthusiasm. And McDonald's. Universal values."
III
The Clusterfuck That Is Dating

Tinder. The T-rexes. The men who were functional and emotionally astute had been snatched up years ago. What remained was trying to eat you. A man with a gravelly voice who finally paid attention like she was worth paying attention to.

"Basically, dating was a walk in the park. Jurassic Park."
IV
The Dumpster Fire That Is Unhealthy Relationships

An open relationship. A labiaplasty. R600 salad tongs. A yellow toothbrush that didn't get to stay. The kind of gaslighting that makes you surprised nothing actually caught fire.

"Open. As in: his pants, the door, and my emotional wound."
V
The Omnishambles That Is Repeating Your Fuckups

The Afrikaner. Cape Town. A wraparound porch she started imagining. A fortnight of easy. A text received mid-Uno. Everything inside her screaming: not again.

"Love is patient. Love is constipated."
VI
The Clanger That Is Learning from Your Mistakes

A solo trip to the Drakensberg that achieved nothing she planned. The internal renovation. Brick by broken brick. Goat shit on the tyres and mascara on the cheeks - and finally, finally free.

"One useful thing the Afrikaner did teach me was the term 'fok voort'. Fuck forward. So forward I fucked, through the rubble of my life."
VII
The Hot Mess That Is Growing Up

Size 14 and fine with it. A daughter who debriefs on the car ride home from her first school social. A front-row seat. The discovery that healing sometimes shows up as a shrug, a laugh, and a step to the left.

"Healing doesn't always have to be a big, soapbox moment. Sometimes it shows up as a casual shrug. A laugh. A step to the left. A twirl. A finger-click. A moment you barely notice, because you're finally present within it."
From the Introduction

A taste of what you're in for.

At 30 years of age, I suddenly find I have a lot to say about life. Not only about my own life, mind you, but other peoples' too. I have opinions on topics as varied as sex, parenting, home décor, and the unforgivable presence of fruit in a savoury salad.

These things have been accumulating since I was squeezed out of utero. Items of specific importance - such as getting myself up the pole at 16, weight gain, navigating single parenting, weight gain, unhealthy relationships, weight gain, and the general state of being a hot mess in my 30s - have, it seems, given me quite a lot to work with.

I need a more genteel platform for my ideas. Like in the olden days, when one might stand on a soapbox in public gardens and share their thoughts with innocent passers-by. But since no one uses actual soapboxes anymore, I figured I'd pull up a virtual one and construct an entire manuscript dedicated to my story.

In the absence of an actual soapbox, I'm going to have to make do with this book.
